# Ascetic Ideals Foreshadowed

The essay's closing sections (§23–25) pivot from genealogy to prescription — or at least, to the announcement that a prescription is needed. But before calling for a new direction, Nietzsche pauses to show that the religious capture of bad conscience was not the *only* possible outcome. The Greeks had gods too — and they used them for the opposite purpose.

## The Greek Alternative (§23)

After §22's "madhouse" — the climax of Christian self-torture — §23 delivers a sharp contrast. "The conception of gods *in itself* need not lead to the degradation of the imagination" (§23). There are "nobler uses for the invention of gods" than self-crucifixion.

The Greek gods served to **ward off bad conscience**, not intensify it. Where the Christian interprets his instincts as sin against God, the Greek interprets his transgressions as **foolishness**:

> "This much even the Greeks of the strongest, bravest age conceded of themselves — 'foolishness,' 'folly,' a little 'disturbance in the head,' this much even the Greeks of the strongest, bravest age conceded of themselves as the reason for much that was bad and calamitous — foolishness, *not* sin! do you grasp that?" (§23)

The Greek gods did not condemn man's nature. Instead, they "served in those days to justify man to a certain extent even in his wickedness, they served as the originators of evil — in those days they took upon themselves, not the punishment but, what is *nobler*, the guilt" (§23).

This is the exact opposite of the Christian mechanism: instead of man projecting guilt onto himself before God, the gods *absorb* the guilt, leaving man free. "He must have been deluded by a *god*," the noble Greeks concluded when confronted with wickedness among their own (§23). The gods provided an explanation that preserved human dignity rather than destroying it.

## Bad Conscience Redirected (§24)

Now Nietzsche makes his prescriptive move. "We modern men are the heirs of the conscience-vivisection and self-torture of millennia: this is what we have practiced longest, it is our distinctive art perhaps, and in any case our subtlety in which we have acquired a refined taste" (§24). Man has had "an 'evil eye' for his natural inclinations" for so long that "they have finally become inseparable from his 'bad conscience'" (§24).

But: "An attempt at the reverse would *in itself* be possible — but who is strong enough for it?" (§24). Instead of wedding bad conscience to natural instincts (as Christianity does), **wed it to the *unnatural* inclinations**: "all those aspirations to the beyond, to that which runs counter to sense, instinct, nature, animal, in short all ideals hitherto, which are one and all hostile to life and ideals that slander the world" (§24).

This is not the abolition of bad conscience but its **redirection**. Don't feel guilty about your animal nature. Feel guilty about your *failure to affirm life*. Turn the machinery of self-condemnation against the very values that created it.

## The "Man of the Future" (§24)

The attainment of this goal would require "a *different* kind of spirit" — one "strengthened by war and victory, for whom conquest, adventure, danger, and even pain have become needs." It would require "habituation to the keen air of the heights, to winter journeys, to ice and mountains in every sense" and "even a kind of sublime wickedness, an ultimate, supremely self-confident mischievousness in knowledge that goes with great health" (§24).

Then Nietzsche names this figure:

> "The *redeeming* man of great love and contempt, the creative spirit whose compelling strength will not let him rest in any aloofness or any beyond, whose isolation is misunderstood by the people as if it were flight *from* reality — while it is only his absorption, immersion, penetration *into* reality... this Antichrist and antinihilist; this victor over God and nothingness — *he must come one day*." (§24)

This figure echoes the [[sovereign-individual]] from §2 — another figure of autonomous power who stands beyond conventional morality. But where the sovereign individual was described in almost psychological terms (self-mastery, proud [[conscience]]), the "man of the future" is a *historical* figure: someone who resolves the crisis that Christianity created. He redeems us "not only from the hitherto reigning ideal but also from that which was bound to grow out of it, from the great nausea, from the will to nothingness, from nihilism" (§24).

## "But What Am I Saying?" (§25)

Nietzsche abruptly breaks off. "But what am I saying? Enough! Enough! At this point it behooves me only to be silent; or I shall usurp that to which only one younger, 'heavier with future,' has a right — that to which only *Zarathustra* has a right, Zarathustra the godless..." (§25).

The essay ends by pointing *beyond itself* — to the Third Essay on ascetic ideals and to *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* as the work that attempts the positive project the genealogy only diagnoses.

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Is the "man of the future" the sovereign individual?** Both figures stand beyond conventional morality. But the sovereign individual appears at the beginning (§2) as the *product* of the morality of mores, while the man of the future appears at the end as the one who must *overcome* what that morality became. Are they the same figure at different points in the argument, or two different visions?

- **Can bad conscience be redirected?** Nietzsche suggests turning bad conscience against the unnatural ideals. But if bad conscience *is* the internalization of instincts (§16), can you use the disease as its own cure? This is the deepest question the essay poses — and doesn't answer.

- **Why the Greek digression?** §23's passage on Greek gods might seem like a tangent after §22's climax. But it does crucial argumentative work: it proves that the religious intensification of guilt was *not inevitable*. The Greeks had gods and bad conscience — they just didn't combine them the way Christianity did. This makes the Christian outcome look like a choice (or an accident), not a necessity.

- **Why break off?** The essay's abrupt ending feels staged. Nietzsche could have continued — he wrote the Third Essay immediately after. The break is a rhetorical device: it creates the sense that what follows (the ascetic ideals) is too dangerous or too big for this essay's frame. The deferral to Zarathustra further distances the *positive* vision from the genealogical analysis.

- **"Sublime wickedness."** The man of the future requires "a kind of sublime wickedness" — mischievousness, great health, love of ice and heights. This is not a moral figure in any recognizable sense. Nietzsche's redeemer is closer to an artist or adventurer than to a saint. What does it mean for the cure to require *wickedness*?
