# Creditor and Debtor

The argumentative engine of the essay. Nietzsche's central genealogical claim is that the moral concepts of [[guilt]], conscience, duty, and obligation originate not in abstract moral reasoning but in the **material relationship between creditor and debtor** — in buying, selling, bartering, and trading. This is where "one person first *measured himself* against another" (§8).

## The Schuld Connection (§4)

The German word *Schuld* means both "guilt" and "debt." Nietzsche argues this is no coincidence — the moral concept of guilt (*Schuld*) "has its origin in the very material concept *Schulden* [debts]" (§4). The genealogists of morals who miss this connection lack "historical instinct" and "second sight."

Against those who derive punishment from "freedom of the will," Nietzsche insists that the creditor-debtor relationship is far older than any concept of free will. The idea that "every injury has its *equivalent* and can actually be paid back" — this is where guilt begins (§4).

## The Debtor's Body as Collateral (§5)

In the archaic contract, the debtor pledges his **body** as security. If he cannot repay, the creditor may claim "his body, his wife, his freedom, or even his life" (§5). In Egypt, the debtor's corpse could find no peace in the grave. The creditor could "inflict every kind of indignity and torture upon the body of the debtor" — cut from it "as much as seemed commensurate with the size of the debt" (§5).

Nietzsche notes that early legal systems contained precise "evaluations, *legal* evaluations, of the individual limbs and parts of the body" — a horrifying calculus of flesh-for-debt.

## Suffering as Payment (§5–6)

The crucial question: what *equivalence* exists between a debt and the creditor's right to inflict suffering? Nietzsche's answer is unsettling: the compensation is not material but psychological. The creditor receives "a kind of *pleasure*" — the pleasure of being allowed to exercise power over the powerless (§5). Making the debtor suffer is itself the payment.

This pleasure is "prized the more highly the more violently it contrasted with the rank and social standing of the creditor" (§6). A low-ranking creditor, through the act of punishing, gets to taste what it feels like to be a master.

## Buying and Selling as the Origin of Thought (§8)

Nietzsche makes his boldest claim: the creditor-debtor relationship is not just the origin of *moral* concepts but of **human reasoning itself**. "Setting prices, determining values, contriving equivalences, exchanging — these preoccupied the earliest thinking of man to so great an extent that in a certain sense they constitute thinking *as such*" (§8).

The oldest kind of astuteness, human pride, the feeling of superiority over other animals — all began here. Man is the "valuating animal *as such*" — the creature that "measures values, evaluates and measures, as the creature that 'valuates'" (§8). Even the word "man" (*manas*) may express this self-understanding.

## Key Quotes

> "Have these genealogists of morals had even the remotest suspicion that, for example, the major moral concept *Schuld* [guilt] has its origin in the very material concept *Schulden* [debts]?" (§4)

> "The equivalence is provided by the creditor's receiving, in place of a literal compensation for an injury... a recompense in the form of a kind of *pleasure*." (§5)

> "Setting prices, determining values, contriving equivalences, exchanging — these preoccupied the earliest thinking of man to so great an extent that in a certain sense they constitute thinking *as such*." (§8)

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Is this a causal or conceptual claim?** Does Nietzsche mean that the debt relationship *historically caused* moral thinking to develop? Or that the conceptual structure of morality (obligation, equivalence, payment) is *borrowed from* the structure of commerce? The essay seems to move between both.

- **The pleasure problem.** The claim that suffering-as-payment provides genuine *equivalence* rests on the reality of [[cruelty-and-festival|pleasure in cruelty]]. If that pleasure is merely an aberration (as modern sensibility would have it), the whole equivalence breaks down. Nietzsche insists it is fundamental — but this is precisely what many readers resist.

- **Where does the creditor-debtor logic end?** The relationship scales from individual contracts (§4–5) to the community as creditor (§9) to ancestors as creditors (§19) to God as maximum creditor (§20). Each step raises the stakes. Is this a single mechanism at different scales, or does something qualitatively change as it enters the religious sphere? See [[guilt-before-god]].
