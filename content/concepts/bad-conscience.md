# Bad Conscience

The essay's central thesis. Bad conscience (*schlechtes Gewissen*) is the **internalization of instincts**: when humans could no longer discharge their drives outward (aggression, cruelty, the pleasure of persecution, change, destruction), those drives turned *inward* against the self. "All instincts that do not discharge themselves outwardly *turn inward*" — this is what Nietzsche calls "the internalization of man" (§16). It is the origin of the "soul," and it is the origin of bad conscience.

## The Mechanism (§16)

Nietzsche's metaphor is vivid: the transition was like sea animals "compelled to become land animals or perish" (§16). Humans who once roamed freely as "semi-animals, well adapted to the wilderness, to war, to prowling, to adventure" were suddenly enclosed within the walls of **society and peace**. "From now on they had to walk on their feet and 'bear themselves' whereas hitherto they had been borne by the water: a dreadful heaviness lay upon them" (§16).

In this new condition, their old instincts were "disvalued and suspended." They were "reduced to their 'consciousness,' their weakest and most fallible organ!" (§16). But the instincts don't vanish — they seek new outlets. "Those fearful bulwarks with which the political organization protected itself against the old instincts of freedom — punishments belong among these bulwarks — brought about that all those instincts of wild, free, prowling man turned backward *against man himself*" (§16).

Hostility, cruelty, the pleasure of persecution, "the joy in sheer change and destruction" — all turn inward. Man begins to attack, persecute, and torment **himself**. The result is an "interior world" that previously had been "as thin as if it were stretched between two membranes" now expanding and gaining "depth, breadth, and height" (§16).

## The Origin of the State (§17)

What caused this sudden confinement? Nietzsche's answer is violent and anti-contractual. The state was not formed by a "contract" — "he who can command, he who is by nature 'master'" does not negotiate. The state was imposed by "some pack of blond beasts of prey, a conqueror and master race which, organized for war... unhesitatingly lays its terrible claws upon a populace perhaps tremendously superior in numbers" (§17).

This is not gradual adaptation but a sudden, overwhelming imposition. The old instincts of freedom are not slowly negotiated away — they are *forcibly suppressed*, all at once, with no outlet. See [[blond-beast]] for the psychology and paradox of the state-founders.

The "state" begins as an "instinctive creation of form" — the conquerors impose form on a formless populace with the "unconscious artistry" of artists working in human material. They are "the most involuntary, unconscious artists" — and their work, "this *instinct for freedom* forcibly made latent," becomes bad conscience (§17).

## Bad Conscience as Illness *and* Pregnancy (§18)

Nietzsche's crucial double valuation: bad conscience is "the gravest and uncanniest illness, from which humanity has not yet recovered" (§16). But it is also an **active force** — "pregnant" with a future.

Bad conscience "brought to light an abundance of strange new beauty and affirmation" — it is "the womb of all *ideal* and *imaginative* phenomena" (§18). Human interiority, the capacity for self-reflection, the entire "interior world" — all are products of this illness.

Nietzsche names the driving force: "the *instinct for freedom* (in my language: the [[will-to-power|will to power]])" (§18). This is one of the essay's most explicit identifications — the instinct for freedom that was "forcibly made latent" by the state (§17) is itself the will to power, and when it can no longer shape the external world, it turns to shaping the self. Bad conscience is the will to power operating on its own possessor.

The suggestion is that bad conscience, though a pathology, is a *creative* pathology. It produces something that didn't exist before: a deep self, capable of ideals, imagination, and (potentially) the kind of self-mastery displayed by the [[sovereign-individual]].

## Key Quotes

> "All instincts that do not discharge themselves outwardly *turn inward* — this is what I call the *internalization* of man: thus it was that man first developed what was later called his 'soul.'" (§16)

> "The entire inner world, originally as thin as if it were stretched between two membranes, expanded and extended itself, acquired depth, breadth, and height, in the same measure as outward discharge was *inhibited*." (§16)

> "I regard the bad conscience as the serious illness that man was bound to contract under the stress of the most fundamental change he ever experienced — that change which occurred when he found himself finally enclosed within the walls of society and of peace." (§16)

> "This secret self-ravishment, this artists' cruelty, this delight in imposing a form upon oneself as a hard, recalcitrant, suffering material... this uncanny, dreadfully joyous labor of a soul voluntarily at odds with itself... had at last — as the father of the 'bad conscience' is — brought to light an abundance of strange new beauty and affirmation." (§18)

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Illness or achievement?** Nietzsche calls bad conscience "the gravest illness" (§16) but also credits it with creating the human soul, ideals, and imagination (§18). Is he condemning or celebrating? Most likely: both. See [[questions/is-bad-conscience-good-or-bad]].

- **Sudden or gradual?** The mechanism in §16 requires a *sudden* suppression of instincts — the imposition of the state in §17 provides this. But was the transition really so abrupt? Nietzsche seems to need the violence and speed for his psychological model to work.

- **Cruelty redirected, not eliminated.** Bad conscience is itself a form of [[cruelty-and-festival|cruelty]] — but directed inward. The instinct for cruelty doesn't disappear; it merely changes its object. This is the through-line from §6–7 (external cruelty) through §16 (self-cruelty) to §22 (religious self-torture). See [[connections/cruelty-sublimated-not-eliminated]].

- **The sovereign individual problem.** If bad conscience creates interiority, and the [[sovereign-individual]] requires profound interiority ("penetrated to the profoundest depths"), then bad conscience is the *precondition* for sovereignty. Does Nietzsche celebrate the fruit (sovereignty) while condemning the tree (bad conscience)? See [[connections/sovereign-individual-vs-bad-conscience]].

- **Blond beasts.** The phrase "blond beast of prey" (*blonde Raubthier*) in §17 has been widely misread as a racial designation. Kaufmann's footnotes clarify that Nietzsche refers to lions — the blond mane — as a metaphor for predatory power. The conquerors are not a specific race but a type: organized, warlike, form-imposing. Still, the passage remains Nietzsche at his most politically provocative.

## Scholarly Commentary

### Leiter: Bad Conscience as Psychological Hypothesis

Leiter (2014: 185-188) emphasizes that §16-18's internalization thesis is a *psychological* claim, not a metaphysical one. Bad conscience is the will to power operating under constraint — a hypothesis about what happens when active drives are blocked. The identification of the "instinct for freedom" with will to power (§18) is a psychological equation, testable against experience: do confined, frustrated organisms turn their aggressive energy inward? Leiter reads this as continuous with the naturalistic program established in §12.

Leiter also identifies an **explanatory gap**: bad conscience alone does not explain how raw self-cruelty became *moralized* guilt. The transformation from self-torture (§16-18) to guilt-before-God (§19-22) requires an additional catalyst — the ascetic priest, whose role is explained only in Essay 3. The Second Essay *describes* the moralization but cannot fully *explain* it without the Third Essay's account. See [[connections/three-essays-unified]].

### Gemes: Interiority Without Personhood

Gemes (2006: 327-332) adds a crucial distinction: bad conscience creates interiority, but interiority alone is not [[personhood-as-achievement|personhood]]. You can have a deep interior world that is still a chaos of competing drives. Bad conscience is a *necessary condition* for genuine selfhood (you need interiority to have a self at all) but not *sufficient* (you also need the hierarchical organization of drives that constitutes a genuine "I"). Most humans who undergo bad conscience remain "jumbles of competing forces" — only the rare individual who imposes order on the chaos achieves the [[sovereign-individual]]'s status.
