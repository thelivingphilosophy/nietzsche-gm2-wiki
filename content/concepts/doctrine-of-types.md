# Doctrine of Types

Leiter's term (*Nietzsche on Morality*, 2014: 8-9, 64-68, 89-91, 126-128) for a thesis central to Nietzsche's philosophy: each person has a relatively fixed psycho-physiological constitution — a set of "type-facts" — that is *causally primary* in determining what they can value, believe, become, and achieve.

## The Core Claim

Natural facts about a person — metabolism, drives, temperament, nervous constitution — circumscribe the range of life-trajectories available to them. These type-facts are:

1. **Necessary** for any given outcome (you cannot become what your constitution forbids)
2. **Not sufficient** — environment, culture, accident also play a role, within the range the type-facts permit (Leiter 2014: 65)

This is not strict determinism but what Leiter calls **Nietzschean Fatalism**: type-facts don't determine every detail of a life, but they set the boundaries. A person's character and personality are "fixed by natural facts, and thus how one responds to differing circumstances and environments is also causally determined by natural facts" (Leiter 2014: 65).

Nietzsche is explicit about this in *Ecce Homo*: "The good fortune of my existence, its uniqueness perhaps, lies in its *fatality* [*Verhängnis*]" (EH I:1). He wrote wise and clever books *because of who he was*, not because he chose to — "It was a lucky fact of nature that I, Nietzsche, was a healthy organism" (EH I:2). The entire self-congratulatory apparatus of *Ecce Homo* is underwritten by fatalism: he instinctively chose the right means because that is what his type does.

## Type-Facts in the Second Essay

The Doctrine of Types operates throughout the essay, though Nietzsche never uses the label:

**§2: The sovereign individual.** The "ripest fruit" of the morality of mores — but not everyone who passes through that process becomes sovereign. Most become merely calculable. Only certain types — those with the right constitution — emerge with genuine autonomy. The sovereign individual is rare because sovereignty requires type-facts most people lack: a unified hierarchy of drives, the capacity for sustained self-binding, the strength to stand alone. See [[sovereign-individual]], [[questions/two-kinds-of-free-will]].

**§9: The community as creditor.** Not everyone responds to communal obligation the same way. The community can afford to let some offenders go unpunished (§10) precisely because the strong community knows its members are different types — some can be reformed, some can't, and mercy is a luxury of power.

**§14-15: Punishment fails.** Punishment doesn't produce guilt because it treats all persons as the same type — as rational agents who will internalize moral lessons. But different types respond differently to the same stimulus. Criminals in prison don't develop conscience because punishment addresses their behavior, not their constitution. See [[punishment]].

**§16-17: Bad conscience.** When the state confines humans within its walls, different types internalize differently. The blond beast type — organized for external conquest — suffers the most violent internalization, because their drive to form-giving is strongest. Weaker types may adapt more easily, having less aggressive instinct to suppress. Bad conscience is most creative, and most pathological, in the strongest types.

**§24: The man of the future.** Nietzsche's "redeeming man" must be a specific type — someone with sufficient strength to redirect bad conscience against life-denying ideals rather than against natural instincts. This is not a universal prescription; it is a call to a particular type of person. The essay ends not with a program for humanity but with a summons to the rare individuals who could carry it out.

## Against Universalism

The Doctrine of Types is why Nietzsche rejects universal morality. MPS's "Similarity" presupposition — that one moral code fits all humans — collapses once you accept that people have fundamentally different constitutions. What is healthy for one type is poison for another (Leiter's "Cornarism" point, 2014: 126-127). Herd morality is genuinely good *for the herd* — but harmful to higher types, who need different conditions for flourishing.

This is not crude elitism but a naturalistic claim: just as different plants require different conditions, different human types require different values. The problem with MPS is not that it exists but that it claims *universality* — "morality says stubbornly and inexorably, 'I am morality *itself*, and nothing besides is morality'" (BGE 202).

## Tensions

- **How fixed are type-facts?** If type-facts are truly causally primary, what room is there for Nietzsche's project of *changing* readers' values? Leiter's answer: the writings can only affect those whose type-facts make them susceptible — "those whose ears are related to ours" (GS 381). Nietzsche addresses "the very few" (A Pref) because only a few have the right type.
- **Is this a scientifically testable claim?** Leiter wants to read Nietzsche as continuous with scientific psychology, but the "type-facts" are never specified precisely enough to be tested. They function more as a philosophical hypothesis than as an empirical program.
- **How does this relate to agency free will?** Gemes (2006) argues that type-facts don't preclude genuine agency — they are the *material* from which a genuine self can be constructed. The sovereign individual doesn't transcend their type-facts but *organizes* them into a coherent hierarchy. See [[questions/two-kinds-of-free-will]].
