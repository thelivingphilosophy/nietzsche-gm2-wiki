# Justice and Law

In §8–11, Nietzsche shifts from the individual creditor-debtor relationship to the **communal** level: the community as creditor, the lawbreaker as debtor to the whole, and the contested question of whether justice originates in reactive feelings (ressentiment) or active force. His answer is emphatic: justice comes from the strong, not the aggrieved.

## The Community as Creditor (§9)

The creditor-debtor relationship (see [[creditor-debtor]]) scales up. The community stands to its members as a creditor to its debtors: you enjoy the benefits of communal life (protection, trust, peace), and in return you are bound by its rules.

The lawbreaker is not just someone who harmed a victim — he is "a breaker of his contract and his word *with the whole*" (§9). He has attacked his creditor. The community's response: it "throws him back again into the savage and outlaw state" — withdrawal of all the benefits and protections he had enjoyed.

"Punishment" at this stage is simply a *mimus* (copy) of the normal hostile attitude toward an enemy: "the rights of war and the victory celebration of the *vae victis!*" (§9).

## Mercy as Self-Overcoming (§10)

As a community grows more powerful, it can afford to be more lenient. Offenses become less threatening; the criminal no longer needs to be expelled. "The creditor always becomes more humane to the extent that he has grown richer" (§10).

Nietzsche's striking claim: a sufficiently powerful society could allow itself "the noblest luxury possible to it — letting those who harm it go *unpunished*." Mercy is not weakness but **self-overcoming of justice** (*Selbstaufhebung*). Justice "ends by winking and letting those incapable of discharging their debt go free."

This "self-overcoming" has a name: **mercy** — "the privilege of the most powerful man, or better, his — *beyond the law*" (§10).

## Against Ressentiment as the Origin of Justice (§11)

This is Nietzsche's polemical core. He targets Eugen Dühring's claim that the origin of justice lies in the reactive feelings — in the injured party's resentment and desire for revenge.

Nietzsche's counter-argument:

1. **The reactive person has a distorted view.** The man of ressentiment has "a false and prejudiced view of the object" — his judgment is always colored by injury. The active, aggressive man "has at all times a *freer* eye, a *better* [[conscience]]" (§11).

2. **Justice requires the *active* stance.** "Being just is always a *positive* attitude" — it requires exaltation, objectivity, and clear-sighted evaluation even under personal assault (§11). This is temperamentally opposite to ressentiment.

3. **Law is the struggle *against* reactive feelings.** "The most decisive act" of the supreme power is the institution of **law** — "the imperative declaration of what in general counts as permitted, as just... and what counts as forbidden, as unjust" (§11). Law takes revenge *out of the hands* of the injured party and replaces it with impersonal evaluation.

4. **"Just" and "unjust" only exist *after* the institution of law** (§11). To speak of justice *in itself* is "senseless" — life operates *essentially* through injury, assault, exploitation, destruction. Legal conditions are always "exceptional conditions" and "partial restrictions" on the will to life.

## Key Quotes

> "The justice which began with 'everything is dischargeable, everything must be discharged,' ends by winking and letting those incapable of discharging their debt go free: it ends, as does every good thing on earth, by *overcoming itself*." (§10)

> "This self-overcoming of justice: one knows the beautiful name it has given itself — *mercy*." (§10)

> "The *last* sphere to be conquered by the spirit of justice is the sphere of the reactive feelings... the just man remains just even toward those who have harmed him." (§11)

> "Wherever justice is practiced and maintained one sees a stronger power seeking a means of putting an end to the senseless raging of *ressentiment*." (§11)

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Is mercy really "beyond" justice?** Nietzsche describes mercy as justice's self-overcoming — but is this an *abolition* of justice or its *highest form*? If the powerful can afford not to punish, does that make mercy a luxury (which could be revoked) or a genuine transcendence?

- **The ressentiment problem.** Nietzsche uses *ressentiment* extensively in the First Essay (slave morality). Here in §11 he insists justice is *not* born from ressentiment. But if the creditor-debtor mechanism generates a desire for compensation (§4–6), isn't that itself a reactive feeling? The distinction between "active desire for compensation" and "reactive ressentiment" may be less clean than Nietzsche suggests.

- **Law as anti-democratic?** Nietzsche's insistence that law originates in the active and powerful — not in the injured party's grievance — has obvious political implications. He explicitly mocks "democratic idiosyncrasy" and "misarchism" (hatred of rule) in §12. Is this a description of how law historically emerged, or a prescription for how it should work?
