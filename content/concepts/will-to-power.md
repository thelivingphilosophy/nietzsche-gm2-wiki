# Will to Power (*Wille zur Macht*)

The will to power is not a separate topic in the Second Essay — it is the **operating principle** running beneath every major argument. Nietzsche never gives it a standalone section the way he does for bad conscience or the creditor-debtor relationship. Instead it surfaces at four critical junctures, each time doing different work. Tracking these appearances reveals the essay's deepest claim: the same force that creates states, generates morality, produces bad conscience, and drives religious self-torture is one force operating under different conditions.

## 1. The Interpretive Principle (§12)

The will to power first appears as a **methodological claim** — a rule for reading history.

Against those who confuse the origin of a thing with its purpose, Nietzsche argues that "all events in the organic world are a subduing, a *becoming master*, and all subduing and becoming master involves a fresh interpretation, an adaptation through which any previous 'meaning' and 'purpose' are necessarily obscured or even obliterated" (§12).

Purposes and utilities are not explanations — they are "only *signs* that a will to power has become master of something less powerful and imposed upon it the character of a function" (§12). The "evolution" of a thing is not progress toward a goal but "a continuous sign-chain of ever new interpretations and adaptations whose causes do not even have to be related to one another."

Against Spencer's "adaptation" as the essence of life: "the essence of life, its *will to power*, is ignored; one overlooks the essential priority of the spontaneous, aggressive, expansive, form-giving forces that give new interpretations and directions" (§12). Adaptation is secondary, reactive — a response to conditions. The will to power is primary, active — the force that *creates* conditions.

**What it does here:** Establishes will to power as the essay's hermeneutic key. Every institution, practice, and concept must be read not as serving a fixed purpose but as the site of successive conquests by forces seeking mastery. This is the framework that makes the eleven meanings of punishment (§13) intelligible — punishment has no essence because its form has been repeatedly seized and reinterpreted by different wills to power. See [[form-vs-meaning]].

## 2. External Form-Giving: The Blond Beast (§17)

The will to power next appears as **political creation** — the violent founding of the state.

The blond beast's state-formation is "an instinctive creation and imposition of forms" — the conquerors are "the most involuntary, unconscious artists there are — wherever they appear something new soon arises, a ruling structure that *lives*, in which parts and functions are delimited and co-ordinated" (§17). They impose form on a "hitherto unchecked and shapeless populace" through pure force.

This is will to power as **external form-giving**: a strong force encounters formless material and shapes it. The shaping is not purposive ("in order to create a state") but instinctive — it is what active power *does*. The blond beast creates structure the way an artist creates a sculpture: because the drive to impose form is irresistible. See [[blond-beast]].

**What it does here:** Shows will to power operating outward — on other people, on raw social material. This sets up the crucial contrast with §18, where the *same* force operates inward.

## 3. Internal Form-Giving: Bad Conscience (§18)

The essay's most explicit identification: **"the *instinct for freedom* (in my language: the will to power)"** (§18).

When the state confines humans within its walls, the instinct for freedom — the will to power — is "forcibly made latent" (§17). But it doesn't disappear. Blocked from shaping the external world, it turns to shaping the self:

"It is the same active force that is at work on a grander scale in those artists of violence and organizers who build states... and that here, internally, on a smaller and pettier scale, directed backward, in the 'labyrinth of the breast,' to use Goethe's expression, creates for itself a bad conscience and builds negative ideals" (§18).

Bad conscience is "this secret self-ravishment, this artists' cruelty, this delight in imposing a form upon oneself as a hard, recalcitrant, suffering material and in burning a will, a critique, a contradiction, a contempt, a No into it" (§18). The will to power, denied its natural objects, takes its own possessor as material.

**What it does here:** Connects the blond beast (§17) and bad conscience (§18) as *the same force* operating in opposite directions. External form-giving creates the state; internal form-giving creates the soul. The conqueror and the self-torturer are driven by one principle. This is why bad conscience is both "illness" and "pregnancy" — the will to power is creative even when it operates self-destructively. See [[bad-conscience]].

## 4. The Will to Self-Torture (§22)

In §22, the will to power reaches its most extreme and self-destructive expression. Bad conscience "seizes upon the presupposition of religion" to drive self-torture to its maximum. But Nietzsche frames this not as passive suffering but as **active will**:

"The *will* of man to find himself guilty and reprehensible to a degree that can never be atoned for; his *will* to think himself punished without any possibility of the punishment becoming equal to the guilt; his *will* to infect and poison the fundamental ground of things with the problem of punishment and guilt" (§22).

The triple emphasis on *will* is deliberate. Religious self-torture is not something that *happens to* man — it is something he *does to himself*, driven by the same force that once built states and imposed form on others. The will to power, completely internalized and given theological justification, now creates an entire cosmology of guilt — "God the Judge, God the Hangman, the beyond, eternity, torment without end, hell, the immeasurability of punishment and guilt" (§22).

**What it does here:** Completes the arc. The same will to power that imposed form on a formless populace (§17) now imposes an infinite guilt on its own possessor (§22). The scale has shifted from political to cosmic, the direction from outward to inward, but the driving force is identical. See [[guilt-before-god]].

## The Full Arc

| Section | Direction | Object | Product |
|---------|-----------|--------|---------|
| §12 | Interpretive principle | History, institutions | Successive reinterpretations |
| §17 | Outward | Other people, raw social material | The state, political form |
| §18 | Inward | The self | Bad conscience, the soul, ideals |
| §22 | Inward + theological | The self before God | Infinite guilt, cosmic self-punishment |

The movement is from **outward and form-giving** to **inward and self-destroying** — but it is *one* force throughout. This is the essay's deepest structural claim: what looks like a series of different phenomena (state-formation, bad conscience, religious guilt) is a single principle operating under successively more restrictive conditions. Each time outward discharge is further blocked, the will to power turns further inward and becomes more self-destructive.

## Will to Power vs Adaptation

§12's polemic against Spencer is not a tangent — it is the theoretical foundation for the whole essay. If adaptation (reactive, adjusting to conditions) were the essence of life, then bad conscience would simply be humanity *adapting* to social confinement. But Nietzsche insists the primary force is active, form-giving, aggressive. Bad conscience is not adaptation — it is the *failure* of the will to power to find adequate external expression, forcing it to turn its creative-destructive energy inward.

This distinction matters for the essay's prescription (§24). If bad conscience were mere adaptation, you could cure it by changing conditions. But if it is the will to power misdirected, the cure must involve *redirecting* the will to power — giving it a new target. Hence §24's call to "wed the bad conscience to all the *unnatural* inclinations" — to turn the will to power against the life-denying ideals rather than against natural instincts. See [[ascetic-ideals-foreshadowed]].

## Leiter's Psychological Reading

Leiter (2014: 112-116, 258-261) argues that the will to power is primarily a **psychological** doctrine, not a metaphysical one. Against commentators who read Nietzsche as claiming that *all* force — animate and inanimate — is will to power, Leiter points out that:

- The strongest textual support for the metaphysical reading (WP 1067: "This world *is* the will to power — and nothing besides!") has been conclusively shown by Montinari to be a discarded notebook passage from 1887, not a settled Nietzschean view
- In the two major self-reflective moments of his career — *Ecce Homo* and the prefaces of 1886 — Nietzsche never mentions will to power as a "fundamental principle"
- Nietzsche repeatedly makes claims incompatible with the strong metaphysical reading (e.g., that will to power can be "lacking" in some organisms, A: 6)
- Clark (1990) argues that the *only* argument for the metaphysical doctrine in Nietzsche's published works (BGE 36) is cast in conditional form and is arguably self-undermining

The will to power is best understood as a psychological hypothesis: the best explanation for a wide range of human behaviors. In Essay 2, it operates as the force driving state-formation (§17), internalization (§18), and religious self-torture (§22) — all psychological phenomena.

This matters for how we read §12's "democratic idiosyncrasy" polemic. The passage is not a metaphysical proclamation that reality itself is will to power. It is a methodological claim: that understanding institutions and practices requires attending to the active, form-giving forces that seize and reinterpret them, rather than treating "adaptation" as primary.

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Is the will to power descriptive or normative?** In §12 it reads as a claim about how things *are* — all events involve will to power. In §18 and §24 it reads as something that can be well or badly directed. If everything is will to power, how can some expressions be better than others? Nietzsche's answer seems to be that outward, form-giving expression is *healthier* than inward, self-destroying expression — but this smuggles in a value judgment that the concept itself doesn't ground.

- **Is bad conscience a *failure* of the will to power?** Or is it the will to power operating *successfully* under constrained conditions? §18 says it creates "an abundance of strange new beauty and affirmation." If self-shaping is genuinely creative, then bad conscience isn't failure — it's the will to power finding the only material available (the self) and working with it. The question is whether the product (interiority, ideals, the soul) justifies the cost (self-torture).

- **Cruelty as the will to power's medium.** The will to power expresses itself through cruelty at every level — external cruelty in §5–7, self-cruelty in §16–18, theological cruelty in §22. Is cruelty *essential* to the will to power, or just the form it takes when blocked? Could there be a non-cruel expression? The essay doesn't say, but the [[sovereign-individual]]'s "proud conscience" (§2) might be an example — will to power expressed as self-mastery rather than self-torture.

- **The "democratic idiosyncrasy."** §12's attack on Spencer and "misarchism" (hatred of rule) connects to a broader cultural critique: the modern democratic instinct resists the idea that a will to power is operating everywhere, because it "opposes everything that dominates and wants to dominate." This makes the will to power not just a philosophical concept but a diagnostic tool for contemporary culture — and a politically charged one.
