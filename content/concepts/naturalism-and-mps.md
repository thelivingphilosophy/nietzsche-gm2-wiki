# Naturalism and Morality in the Pejorative Sense (MPS)

Brian Leiter's *Nietzsche on Morality* (2nd ed., 2014) provides the most systematic framework for understanding what Nietzsche is doing in the *Genealogy*. Two concepts are central: Nietzsche's **naturalism** — explaining moral phenomena in terms of psychology and physiology — and his target, **morality in the pejorative sense** (MPS).

## Nietzsche as Naturalist

Leiter argues that Nietzsche is a **methodological naturalist**: he holds that human beings, including their moral beliefs and practices, can and should be explained in *scientific* terms — specifically, in terms of what Leiter calls "type-facts" about persons (psycho-physiological facts about character, drives, constitution). Nietzsche's naturalism does not commit him to reductive materialism; it commits him to the explanatory priority of natural facts over supernatural, metaphysical, or purely rational explanations (Leiter 2014: 1-23).

The *Genealogy* is naturalism in action. Each essay takes a moral phenomenon that appears self-evident or divinely ordained — slave morality, guilt, the ascetic ideal — and shows it to be the product of identifiable psychological mechanisms operating under specific historical conditions.

In the Second Essay, naturalism operates through:
- **§1-3**: The "breeding" of a calculable animal through pain — a physiological account of how memory and promising arose
- **§4-8**: The creditor-debtor relationship as the naturalistic origin of moral concepts like guilt, duty, and obligation
- **§12-13**: The form-vs-meaning principle as a naturalistic method — understanding institutions by tracing the successive forces that have seized and reinterpreted them
- **§16-18**: Bad conscience as the naturalistic result of instinct-internalization under social confinement

## MPS: Nietzsche's Target

Leiter argues Nietzsche does not attack *all* morality — he explicitly endorses a "higher morality" for higher types. His target is a specific kind of morality, which Leiter calls **MPS**: morality characterized by three descriptive presuppositions and a distinctive normative agenda.

### The Three Descriptive Presuppositions

MPS depends for its intelligibility on three claims about human agency (Leiter 2014: 63):

1. **Free Will**: Human agents possess a will capable of free and autonomous choice
2. **Transparency of the Self**: Agents' actions can be sufficiently distinguished on the basis of their respective motives
3. **Similarity**: Human agents are sufficiently similar that one moral code is appropriate for all

Nietzsche denies all three. His **fatalism** holds that type-facts about persons are causally primary — they circumscribe what a person can become, making universal moral codes inapplicable to different types (Leiter 2014: 64-66). His attack on free will (GM I:13, the "doer behind the deed" passage) removes the basis for moral responsibility in the deserts sense. His emphasis on rank-ordering and the rarity of the [[sovereign-individual]] denies human similarity.

### The Normative Component

MPS embraces norms that harm the flourishing of "higher men" while benefiting the "lowest" — altruism, equality, pity, selflessness. Nietzsche's critique is that these norms, which arose from the reactive psychology of *ressentiment* (Essay 1), are presented as universal and self-evident when they are in fact the self-interested creation of a particular type of person.

## MPS and the Second Essay

The Second Essay traces how MPS's descriptive presuppositions were *constructed*:

- **§1-3** show the immense labor required to make humans calculable — proving that promise-keeping is not natural but artificially bred. The [[sovereign-individual]] who can genuinely promise is the exception, not the rule
- **§4-8** show that moral concepts (guilt, duty, conscience) originate not in metaphysical freedom but in the material relationship of creditor and debtor — "the oldest and most primitive personal relationship, that between buyer and seller" (§8)
- **§13** demonstrates that punishment has no single meaning — undermining the idea that it serves a fixed moral purpose
- **§14-15** argue that punishment *hinders* rather than produces guilt — further dismantling the MPS picture in which punishment produces moral improvement
- **§16-18** show that bad conscience — the internalization of instincts — has a naturalistic, not moral, origin: the violent confinement of humans within the walls of the state

The upshot: MPS presents itself as describing timeless moral truths about free, responsible, equal agents. The *Genealogy* shows that every element of this picture — the concepts, the institutions, the self-understanding — was historically produced by forces that had nothing to do with morality.

## Leiter's Anti-Realism

Leiter argues (2014: 118-131) that Nietzsche is an **anti-realist** about moral value: there are no objective moral facts. Moral values are "images and fantasies," projective errors akin to attributing gender to the sun. Nietzsche's central argument is *explanatory*: since we can fully explain why people hold the moral beliefs they do by appealing to type-facts about them, we need not posit objective moral facts.

This matters for the Second Essay because it reframes the entire genealogical project: Nietzsche is not showing that our moral concepts have the *wrong* origin (a genetic fallacy). He is showing that they have a *complete natural explanation*, which removes any reason to think they track mind-independent moral reality.

## Key Quotes
