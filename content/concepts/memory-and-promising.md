# Memory and Promising

The essay opens with a paradox: how do you breed an animal *with the right to make promises*? This is "the real problem regarding man" (§1). Nietzsche's answer unfolds through three interlocking concepts: active forgetfulness, the memory of the will, and mnemotechnics — the brutal technology by which humans were made calculable.

## Active Forgetfulness (*Vergesslichkeit*)

Nietzsche redefines forgetfulness not as passive failure but as an **active faculty of repression** (§1). It is a doorkeeper, a preserver of "psychic order, repose, and etiquette." Without it, there could be "no happiness, no cheerfulness, no hope, no pride, no *present*." Forgetfulness is a sign of robust health — the organism's ability to digest experience and move on.

This is the first of many inversions in the essay: what we normally consider a deficiency (forgetting) is actually a positive capacity, and what we consider a virtue (memory, responsibility) required violent intervention to produce.

## Memory of the Will (*Gedächtnis des Willens*)

Against this healthy forgetfulness, humans have bred "an opposing faculty, a *memory*" — but not ordinary recall. This is a **memory of the will**: the ability to hold an intention across time, to bridge the gap between "I will" and "I do" despite everything that intervenes (§1). This is what makes promising possible.

To ordain the future, man must first become "calculable, regular, *necessary*" — predictable even to himself. The "tremendous labor" of the "morality of mores" (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*) achieved exactly this: it made man *actually calculable* through custom, tradition, and social pressure (§2).

## Mnemotechnics: Pain as Memory Aid

"How can one create a memory for the human animal?" (§3). Nietzsche's answer is blunt: **pain**. "If something is to stay in the memory it must be *burned* in: only that which never ceases to *hurt* stays in the memory" (§3).

He catalogs the gruesome history: stoning, breaking on the wheel, piercing with stakes, quartering, boiling in oil, flaying alive. These are not aberrations — they are the *mnemotechnics* by which civilization inscribed its demands into human flesh. "With the aid of such images and procedures one finally remembers five or six 'I will not's'" (§3).

The worse man's memory, the more fearful the punishments. The severity of penal codes is "an especially significant measure of the degree of effort needed to overcome forgetfulness" (§3).

## Key Quotes

> "To breed an animal *with the right to make promises* — is not this the paradoxical task that nature has set itself in the case of man?" (§1)

> "Forgetting is no mere *vis inertiae* as the superficial imagine; it is rather an active and in the strictest sense positive faculty of repression." (§1)

> "If something is to stay in the memory it must be *burned* in: only that which never ceases to *hurt* stays in the memory — this is a main clause of the oldest (unhappily also the most enduring) psychology on earth." (§3)

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Is forgetfulness better than memory?** Nietzsche clearly values active forgetfulness as healthy, but the whole essay traces how memory-making (however brutal) produced human interiority, culture, and ultimately the [[sovereign-individual]]. The question is whether the product justifies the process.
- **Mnemotechnics vs. bad conscience.** The pain-techniques of §3 are *external* — inflicted by society on the individual. But [[bad-conscience]] (§16) involves the individual inflicting pain on *themselves*. These are two different mechanisms, and Nietzsche is careful not to conflate them, even though both involve suffering as a formative force.
- **The morality of mores paradox.** The "morality of mores" makes man calculable, but the [[sovereign-individual]] is described as "liberated *again* from morality of custom" (§2). The tool that shapes him is also what he transcends.

## Scholarly Commentary

### Leiter: Naturalistic Breeding, Not Moral Education

Leiter (2014: 180-183) emphasizes that §1-3 describe a **naturalistic process** — breeding through pain, not rational moral education. The morality of custom (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*) works on the body, not the mind: "perhaps there was nothing more fearful and uncanny in the whole prehistory of man than his mnemotechnics" (§3). The point is that calculability is *not* a natural human capacity — it had to be physically installed through violence. This is a direct application of Nietzsche's [[naturalism-and-mps|naturalism]]: moral capacities are explained by natural processes, not by rational insight or divine endowment.

Leiter also connects §1-3 to the [[doctrine-of-types]]: the morality of mores produces different results in different types. Most humans become merely calculable — conformist promise-keepers backed by external enforcement. Only certain types, with the right constitution, emerge as [[sovereign-individual|sovereign individuals]]. The process is universal; the outcome is not.

### Gemes: Calculability vs. Genuine Promising

Gemes (2006: 327) draws a sharp distinction between the calculability the morality of mores produces and the genuine promising the sovereign individual achieves. Calculability is a capacity for conformist behavior — keeping promises because the system enforces them. Genuine promising requires a [[personhood-as-achievement|genuine self]] — a stable "I" that persists across time. The morality of mores creates the first; only the rare integration of drives creates the second.
