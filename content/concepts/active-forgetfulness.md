# Active Forgetfulness (*Hemmungsvermögen*)

The essay's first move is a rehabilitation: forgetfulness is not a defect but a **positive faculty of repression** (*Positives Hemmungsvermögen*). Nietzsche insists it is "no mere *vis inertiae* as the superficial imagine" — not passive inertia, not a failure of mind, but an active power that governs what enters consciousness and what is turned away (§1).

## The Concept

Active forgetfulness is the organism's doorkeeper. What we experience and absorb passes through consciousness "as little while we are digesting it" as physical nourishment does — a process Nietzsche calls "inpsychation" or "incorporation." Forgetfulness closes the doors and windows of consciousness: it makes room for new things, preserves "psychic order, repose, and etiquette," and allows the organism to live in a genuine present.

Without it, there could be "no happiness, no cheerfulness, no hope, no pride, no *present*." Forgetfulness is the precondition of health.

## The Dyspeptic

Nietzsche's diagnostic image for its failure is the dyspeptic: the man in whom "this apparatus of repression is damaged and ceases to function properly" cannot "have done" with anything. He cannot digest experience; everything accumulates; nothing is finished. The parallel to physical digestion is exact — blocked instinct, blocked processing, blocked discharge. The dyspeptic is the psychic counterpart of constipation.

This figure quietly anticipates [[bad-conscience]]: by §16, the *entire human animal* has become dyspeptic, its instincts blocked from outward discharge and forced inward. Bad conscience is what happens when active forgetfulness fails at a civilizational scale.

## Against Memory

Active forgetfulness stands in direct contrast to the *Gedächtnis des Willens* — the memory of the will — which is the counter-faculty bred into humans to make promising possible (§1). Memory is not forgetfulness's natural opposite; it is a violent imposition against it. See [[memory-and-promising]].

The polarity is:

| | Active Forgetfulness | Memory of the Will |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Positive, organic, primary | Bred, artificial, counter-natural |
| **Function** | Closes consciousness; digests | Holds the will open across time |
| **Affect** | Health, presence, cheerfulness | Calculability, reliability, seriousness |
| **Requires** | Nothing — the default | Mnemotechnics: pain burned into flesh |

## Key Quotes

> "Forgetting is no mere *vis inertiae* as the superficial imagine; it is rather an active and in the strictest sense positive faculty of repression, that is responsible for the fact that what we experience and absorb enters our consciousness as little while we are digesting it." (§1)

> "To close the doors and windows of consciousness for a time; to remain undisturbed by the noise and struggle of our underworld of utility organs working with and against one another; a little quietness, a little *tabula rasa* of the consciousness, to make room for new things..." (§1)

> "The man in whom this apparatus of repression is damaged and ceases to function properly may be compared (and more than merely compared) with a dyspeptic — he cannot 'have done' with anything." (§1)

## The Freud Connection

The German behind Kaufmann's "faculty of repression" is *Positives Hemmungsvermögen* — positive inhibitory capacity. The word *Hemmung* (inhibition, blocking) sits squarely in the conceptual neighborhood of Freud's *Verdrängung* (repression), developed in the 1890s–1900s. The structural parallel is striking: both concepts describe a psychic mechanism that regulates what reaches consciousness and what does not.

The critical difference is valuation. Nietzsche's *Hemmungsvermögen* is **healthy** — a sign of robust life, the organism functioning as it should. Freud's *Verdrängung* is characteristically **pathological** — the exclusion of threatening material that then returns as symptom, dream, or parapraxis.

Freud's own relationship to Nietzsche was ambivalent and well-documented. He told his biographer Ernest Jones that he had "denied himself the great pleasure of reading Nietzsche, with the deliberate purpose of not being hampered in working out the impressions received in psychoanalysis by any preconceived ideas." The confession is itself significant: avoidance as a defense against influence testifies to recognition that the influence was real. Freud also wrote that Nietzsche "had a more penetrating knowledge of himself than any other man who ever lived or was likely to live." In 1908 the Vienna Psychoanalytic Society devoted sessions to discussing Nietzsche's works, with Freud present. The historian of psychoanalysis Paul-Laurent Assoun (*Freud and Nietzsche*, 1980) documents the parallels in detail, arguing that Nietzsche provided the conceptual vocabulary that Freud then regrounded in a clinical and economic framework.

Whether Freud derived *Verdrängung* from *Hemmungsvermögen* directly is unprovable. What is clear is that Nietzsche identified the same phenomenon first — and, characteristically, valued it the opposite way.

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Repression as health.** In Freud the word "repression" carries almost entirely negative connotations; in Nietzsche it names the organism's proper functioning. The same concept, inverted.
- **Can you want forgetfulness?** Nietzsche presents active forgetfulness as organic and pre-reflective — it simply works, or it doesn't. It cannot be willed into existence (that would require memory). The sovereign individual may represent someone who has recovered something *like* it — but whether this is the same forgetfulness or a higher analogue is unclear.
- **Forgetfulness and depth.** The connection page [[connections/forgetfulness-vs-bad-conscience]] tracks the paradox: everything Nietzsche values in human interiority (depth, soul, imagination) was produced precisely by forgetfulness *failing*. A creature of pure active forgetfulness would be healthy but thin — "as thin as if stretched between two membranes."
