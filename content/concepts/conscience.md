# Conscience (*Gewissen*)

Nietzsche uses *Gewissen* (conscience) and *schlechtes Gewissen* (bad conscience) as sharply distinct concepts. Bad conscience is the essay's famous pathology — instincts turned against the self (§16). But *conscience without the adjective* appears several times in the essay as something entirely different: a healthy, affirmative instinct. Tracking this distinction reveals a layer of the argument that's easy to miss.

As Kaufmann's footnote on the essay's title clarifies: "*Schlechtes Gewissen* is no technical term but simply the common German equivalent of 'bad conscience'... *Gewissen*, like conscience, and unlike the French *conscience*, cannot mean consciousness" (p. 493n). The word means specifically *moral self-awareness* — and Nietzsche gives it three distinct valences across the essay.

## 1. The Sovereign Individual's Conscience (§2)

The sovereign individual — autonomous, supramoral, master of a free will — feels "the proud awareness of the extraordinary privilege of *responsibility*, the consciousness of this rare freedom, this power over oneself and over fate." This awareness "has in his case penetrated to the profoundest depths and become instinct, the dominating instinct." What does he call it?

> "The answer is beyond doubt: this sovereign man calls it his *conscience*." (§2)

This is conscience as **proud self-mastery** — not guilt, not anxiety, not the voice that says "you shouldn't have done that." It is the instinct by which a genuinely autonomous person holds *themselves* to their own standard. It is an instinct of *power*, not of punishment. See [[sovereign-individual]].

§3 immediately acknowledges how strange this sounds: "His conscience? — It is easy to guess that the concept of 'conscience' that we here encounter in its highest, almost astonishing, manifestation, has a long history and variety of forms behind it." The sovereign individual's conscience is the "ripest fruit" — the latest, rarest product of a long and violent developmental process. It is conscience in its "highest" form, which means there are lower forms.

## 2. The Good Conscience of Cruelty (§7)

Earlier in human history, cruelty needed no justification. Making others suffer was openly enjoyed — as festival, as spectacle, as an ingredient of "almost every one of their pleasures" (§7). Humans posited cruelty "as a matter of principle" as "a normal quality of man — and thus as something to which the conscience cordially says *Yes!*" (§7).

This is the **good conscience of the predator**: no inner conflict, no guilt, no separation between instinct and self-judgment. The conscience affirms what the instincts want. It is the opposite of bad conscience, where the conscience *condemns* what the instincts want.

Nietzsche is not nostalgic for this — his tone is diagnostic, not wistful. But he insists on the historical reality: for most of human history, cruelty was practiced with a clear conscience, and the idea that cruelty requires justification or produces guilt is modern and anomalous. See [[cruelty-and-festival]].

## 3. The "Better Conscience" of the Active Type (§11)

In his digression against deriving justice from ressentiment, Nietzsche draws a direct contrast:

> "The aggressive man, as the stronger, nobler, more courageous, has in fact also had at all times a *freer* eye, a *better conscience* on his side: conversely, one can see who has the invention of the 'bad conscience' on his conscience — the man of *ressentiment!*" (§11)

The active, aggressive person acts with a **clear conscience** — a "better conscience" (*besseres Gewissen*). They have "a freer eye" because they don't need to falsify what they see. The reactive person, by contrast, *invents* bad conscience — projects guilt onto the world because they cannot act.

This is conscience as a function of **strength**: the strong act and judge clearly; the weak, unable to act, develop the inward-turning self-torment that Nietzsche will anatomize in §16. The "better conscience" is not reflective self-mastery (that's §2's version); it's pre-reflective self-alignment. The active type simply *is* what they do, without the gap between instinct and judgment that bad conscience opens up.

## The Three Consciences

| Type | Section | What it is | Relation to instinct |
|------|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| Sovereign conscience | §2 | Proud self-mastery; the right to make promises | Instincts integrated under a dominant will |
| Good conscience of cruelty | §7 | Pre-reflective affirmation of natural drives | Instincts unopposed by any inner tribunal |
| Better conscience of the active type | §11 | Clear-eyed action without self-falsification | Instincts discharged outward, no inward turn |

All three share a common structure: **no gap between instinct and self-judgment**. In each case, the person acts and the conscience affirms the action (or, in the sovereign's case, the conscience *is* the capacity for self-binding action). Bad conscience, by contrast, is precisely the *opening* of that gap — the instincts want one thing, the conscience condemns it, and the resulting tension becomes self-torture.

## The Structural Puzzle: Before and After Bad Conscience

The essay introduces conscience (§2–3) *before* it introduces bad conscience (§16). This is not just chronological ordering — it's argumentative. Conscience in its "highest" form (the sovereign individual) appears as the essay's opening vision. Bad conscience (the internalization of instincts) appears as the dark explanation for how interiority — and thus conscience — became possible at all.

This creates a genealogical paradox:

- The sovereign individual's conscience (§2) is the "ripest fruit" of the process that *includes* bad conscience (§16)
- Bad conscience is what creates the interior world, the "depth, breadth, and height" that the sovereign's self-mastery presupposes
- The good conscience of §7 is *pre*-bad-conscience: humans before internalization, acting without inner conflict
- The sovereign's conscience is *post*-bad-conscience: a person who has passed through internalization and come out the other side with self-mastery

So the developmental arc is: **good conscience (§7) → bad conscience (§16) → sovereign conscience (§2)**. The essay presents this out of order — §2 first, then §7, then §16 — which means the reader encounters the endpoint before the mechanism. Whether this is a deliberate rhetorical strategy or a sign of the essay's non-linear structure is itself a question. See [[questions/does-the-essay-have-a-coherent-argument]].

## Conscience and the "Sting" (*morsus conscientiae*) (§15)

Spinoza's *morsus conscientiae* — the "sting of conscience" — appears in §15 as something different again. For Spinoza it is "the opposite of *gaudium*": "a sadness accompanied by the recollection of a past event that flouted all of our expectations" (§15). Nietzsche uses Spinoza to show that even the "sting" of conscience is not moral guilt — it is *regret at miscalculation*. "Something has unexpectedly gone wrong," not "I ought not to have done that."

This reinforces the broader point: conscience *as such* is not about guilt. Guilt is what happens when conscience gets infected by bad conscience — when the self-judging faculty turns against the self's own instincts rather than affirming them.

## Tensions and Ambiguities

- **Is the sovereign's conscience continuous with or discontinuous from the "good conscience" of §7?** The good conscience of cruelty is pre-reflective — no inner world, no self-judgment. The sovereign's conscience is maximally reflective — "penetrated to the profoundest depths." These seem like different things that share a name. But both involve the absence of inner conflict. Is the sovereign individual's achievement to recover, at a higher level, the self-alignment that the blond beast had naively?

- **Can you have conscience without bad conscience?** If bad conscience creates interiority, and the sovereign's conscience *requires* interiority, then bad conscience is a necessary precondition for genuine conscience. The disease is the condition of the cure. This is Nietzsche at his most Hegelian — though he wouldn't appreciate the comparison.

- **Why does Nietzsche use the same word?** Conscience (*Gewissen*) and bad conscience (*schlechtes Gewissen*) share a root, and Nietzsche lets the ambiguity stand. Is he saying bad conscience is a *corruption* of conscience? A *stage* of conscience? Or that they are fundamentally different things that happen to share a word — the way *Schuld* means both guilt and debt?

- **Where does conscience go after §3?** The sovereign individual's conscience appears in §2–3 and essentially vanishes. The rest of the essay is dominated by bad conscience. Does conscience-as-self-mastery survive somewhere in the essay's later sections, or is it swallowed by the pathology? The §24 "man of the future" who redeems us may be the sovereign individual returned — but Nietzsche doesn't say.

## Gemes: Conscience as Evidence of Genuine Selfhood

Gemes (2006: 327-332) reads the sovereign individual's conscience not merely as "proud self-mastery" but as evidence of a **genuine self** — a unified hierarchy of drives constituting a real person. The sovereign's conscience is the felt awareness that one *is* a person: that there is a stable "I" behind one's actions, capable of binding itself across time. This is what Gemes calls "agency free will" — not the metaphysical fiction of uncaused choice, but the psychological reality of acting from a coherent character.

On this reading, most humans *lack* genuine conscience in §2's sense — not because they are immoral, but because they don't have a unified self whose mastery could become an instinct. Their "conscience" is borrowed: the external voice of custom, religion, or social pressure internalized as a substitute for genuine self-knowledge. The sovereign individual's conscience is *his own* because it arises from a real self, not from a borrowed framework. See [[personhood-as-achievement]].
