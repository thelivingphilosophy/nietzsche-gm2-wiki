# The Doer and the Deed

GM I:13 contains Nietzsche's most famous attack on the metaphysical subject: "there is no 'being' behind doing, effecting, becoming; 'the doer' is merely a fiction added to the deed — the doing is everything." This passage casts a long shadow over the Second Essay, where the sovereign individual is described as "master of a free will" (§2). The apparent contradiction has become a central site of scholarly debate.

## The Passage (GM I:13)

Nietzsche's argument proceeds through the lambs-and-birds-of-prey parable. Lambs resent eagles for eating them, but it is "absurd" to demand that "strength should *not* express itself as strength." There is no neutral subject behind the eagle's action who *chooses* to be predatory:

> "Popular morality separates strength from manifestations of strength, as though there were an indifferent substratum behind the strong person which had the *freedom* to manifest strength or not. But there is no such substratum; there is no 'being' behind doing, effecting, becoming; 'the doer' is merely a fiction added to the deed."

The weak *need* this fiction: it lets them recast their powerlessness as a moral *choice* ("we choose to be meek") and hold the strong *accountable* for being strong ("they could stop if they wanted to").

## Three Readings

### Leiter: Straightforward Denial of the Subject

Leiter (2014: 88-90, 172) reads I:13 as a flat denial of the metaphysical subject, consistent with Nietzsche's fatalism. There is no "doer" behind the deed — actions flow from type-facts, not from a neutral agent choosing among options. The sovereign individual's "free will" is either ironic or describes the expression of favorable type-facts, not genuine self-determination. On this reading, both I:13 and II:2 are consistent because neither involves genuine choice — the sovereign individual acts from who he *is*, not from some uncaused act of will.

### Gemes: Genealogical Attack on Deserts Free Will Only

Gemes (2006: 328-330) argues I:13 attacks only **deserts free will** — the notion that there is a neutral subject who "could have done otherwise" and therefore deserves punishment or reward. The passage does *not* deny that some individuals have genuine agency — a unified self that acts from character. The fiction I:13 exposes is not selfhood as such but the *neutral, indifferent* subject that slave morality requires in order to assign blame.

On this reading, the sovereign individual has a genuine self — a unified hierarchy of drives that constitutes a real agent — and I:13 is compatible with II:2 because they are about different things: I:13 denies the metaphysical substrate; II:2 affirms the psychological achievement of genuine character.

### Janaway: Genealogical Diagnosis, Not Metaphysical Argument

Janaway (2006: 344-347) argues I:13 is doing genealogy, not metaphysics. Nietzsche is not arguing that free will doesn't exist — he is asking what *affective states* drive the belief in a neutral subject. The answer: *ressentiment*. The weak need the fiction of the free agent because they need a target for blame. Without a "doer" who "could have done otherwise," there is no one to hold accountable, and ressentiment has no object.

This is a genealogical explanation of *why* people believe in the metaphysical subject, not a proof that agency doesn't exist. Janaway connects this to Bernard Williams's observation that the concept of the free agent arose from "a felt need, closely associated with the feeling of *ressentiment*, to locate an absolute responsibility in the other" (Williams 1994: 245).

## Consequences for the Second Essay

The doer-and-deed question reverberates through several moments in Essay 2:

**§2: The sovereign individual.** If there is no doer behind the deed, how can the sovereign individual be "master of a free will"? The answer depends on which reading of I:13 you accept. For Leiter, the sovereign individual's "mastery" is the expression of type-facts. For Gemes, it is genuine agency achieved by the rare person who has unified their drives. For Janaway, it is a future possibility — what agency might look like after we cast off the moralized fiction.

**§13-15: Punishment.** Punishment presupposes a "doer" who could have done otherwise and therefore deserves retribution. If the doer is a fiction, punishment's retributive justification collapses — which is exactly what Nietzsche argues in §14-15. Punishment doesn't produce guilt because it addresses a fiction (the free agent) rather than the actual person (a constitution that acts from necessity).

**§14: The criminal's perspective.** The criminal subjected to punishment sees "the same kind of actions practiced for the purpose of justice" — judges who spy, deceive, and use violence. The criminal cannot see his own deed as inherently different from what is done to him, because neither he nor the judge acts from metaphysical freedom. Both act from what they are.

**§16-18: Bad conscience.** If there is no doer behind the deed, then bad conscience is not self-*blame* (which requires a doer who could have done otherwise) but self-*cruelty* — instincts turned against the organism, with no moral judgment involved. The moralization of bad conscience into guilt (§19-22) then represents the *imposition* of the doer-fiction onto the raw material of self-cruelty: you feel terrible, and religion tells you it's because you — a free agent — chose to sin.

> "There is no 'being' behind doing, effecting, becoming; 'the doer' is merely a fiction added to the deed — the doing is everything." (GM I:13)
