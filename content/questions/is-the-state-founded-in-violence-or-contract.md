# Is the State Founded in Violence or Contract?

## The Question

Nietzsche flatly rejects the social contract tradition. The state was not negotiated into existence by free individuals calculating their self-interest. It was imposed by "some pack of blond beasts of prey" — conquerors who "unhesitatingly lay their terrible claws upon a populace perhaps tremendously superior in numbers" (§17). There was no consent.

Is he right? And does it matter for the rest of the essay's argument?

## Nietzsche's Claim

"I employed the word 'state': it is obvious what is meant — some pack of blond beasts of prey, a conqueror and master race which, organized for war... unhesitatingly lays its terrible claws upon a populace perhaps tremendously superior in numbers but still formless and nomad" (§17).

He is emphatic: "He who can command, he who is by nature 'master,' he who is violent in act and bearing — what has he to do with contracts!" You don't *negotiate* with the material you shape. The state-founders are "unconscious artists" who impose form on human material the way a sculptor works clay — except the material is flesh, and the tool is force.

## Why It Matters for the Essay

Nietzsche's entire theory of [[bad-conscience]] depends on the *suddenness* and *violence* of this transition. Bad conscience arises because instincts are blocked *all at once* — "like sea animals compelled to become land animals or perish" (§16). If state formation were gradual (as social contract theory implies), the instincts would have time to adapt, and the catastrophic inward turning wouldn't occur.

The argument requires:
1. A sharp boundary — before/after, not a continuum
2. External imposition — not voluntary self-restriction
3. No negotiation — the [[blond-beast|blond beasts]] don't ask

If any of these conditions weaken, the mechanism of bad conscience becomes less plausible. Gradual, consensual state formation would produce gradual adaptation, not the explosive internalization Nietzsche describes.

## The Contract Tradition's Response

Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau each tell different versions of the social contract story, but they share a core claim: the state's legitimacy rests on (at least hypothetical) consent. Even if actual states were founded through conquest, we can evaluate them by asking whether rational individuals *would* have agreed to them.

Against Nietzsche, the contractarian might argue:
- **Historical accuracy isn't the point.** Social contract theory is normative (about legitimacy), not descriptive (about origins). Nietzsche is answering a question nobody asked.
- **Conquest still requires cooperation.** Even the "pack of blond beasts" had to organize themselves — they had internal contracts, hierarchies, shared commitments. The state isn't pure imposition all the way down.
- **Gradualism is more plausible.** Archaeological and anthropological evidence generally supports gradual state formation through increasing social complexity, not a single conquest event.

## Nietzsche's Possible Counter

Nietzsche's [[form-vs-meaning]] method (§12) would likely dismiss the historical objection: even if actual state formation was gradual, the *psychological mechanism* of bad conscience requires thinking about it as a rupture. The genealogy is a reconstruction of psychological dynamics, not a historical chronicle. What matters is the *structure* of the event (sudden confinement of instinct), not its literal calendar.

And on legitimacy: Nietzsche would likely view the contractarian concern with legitimacy as itself a product of the moralized guilt the essay diagnoses — the need to justify power rather than simply exercising it. The [[blond-beast|blond beasts]] don't need legitimacy. They act.

## The Psychological Core

Strip away the historiography and Nietzsche's claim reduces to: **the transition from freedom to social constraint was experienced as violent, not voluntary.** Whether it literally involved a conquering horde or happened through slower mechanisms, the subjective experience was one of forcible suppression.

This is testable against individual experience: think about the first time you were forced to suppress a strong impulse (anger, desire, aggression) in a social context. Did it feel like a negotiated agreement, or like something imposed on you from outside?

## Discussion Angles

- Does it matter whether the state was *actually* founded through conquest, or is Nietzsche making a psychological point about how social constraint *feels*?
- The "unconscious artists" passage (§17) aestheticizes political violence. Is this a genuine insight (state-building is a creative act) or a dangerous romanticization?
- If the state requires violence to exist, can it ever be fully legitimate? Or is legitimacy always a retrospective justification?
- Think about an institution you belong to (a company, a school, a community). Was your entry voluntary? Or was there an element of "unhesitatingly laying terrible claws"?
