# Why Attack Ressentiment Here?

## The Question

In §11, Nietzsche suddenly devotes significant space to attacking the idea that justice originates in *ressentiment* — the reactive feelings of the injured party. This seems like a digression from the main thread about guilt, debt, and punishment. Why does he interrupt the genealogy of guilt to make this argument? What work does it do in the essay's overall structure?

## The Surface Reading: It's a Polemic Against Dühring

Nietzsche is responding to Eugen Dühring, who argued that the origin of justice lies in the reactive feeling of being wronged. Nietzsche names him explicitly and rebuts him directly. On this reading, §11 is simply a scholarly dispute — an excursus that could be skipped without losing the main argument.

## The Deeper Reading: It Protects the Genealogy

§11 does crucial work for the essay's larger architecture:

### 1. It establishes the active/reactive distinction

Nietzsche needs to distinguish between **active** and **reactive** forces — this distinction underpins everything from the [[form-vs-meaning]] method (§12) to the theory of [[bad-conscience]] (§16). If justice were born from reaction (ressentiment), then all of morality would be reactive. By insisting that justice originates in the *active* — in the strong imposing order — Nietzsche preserves the possibility of *active* moral creation.

### 2. It sets up §12's method

Immediately after §11, Nietzsche introduces his [[form-vs-meaning]] principle: origin ≠ purpose, and all "becoming master" involves active imposition. The anti-ressentiment argument in §11 is the *first application* of this principle (before it's formally stated): justice didn't *originate* in revenge, even if it later *incorporated* revenge-like functions.

### 3. It differentiates bad conscience from ressentiment

In the First Essay, Nietzsche argued that slave morality arises from *ressentiment*. In this Second Essay, bad conscience arises from the *internalization of instincts* — a different mechanism entirely. The §11 digression helps keep these two pathologies distinct: ressentiment is reactive (directed outward at an oppressor), while bad conscience is active (directed inward at oneself).

### 4. It addresses a potential objection

If guilt originates in the [[creditor-debtor]] relationship (§4–8), a reader might reasonably conclude: "So guilt is just resentment repackaged — the creditor's anger at being unpaid." §11 blocks this reading. The creditor's desire for compensation is *active* (a positive exercise of power), not reactive (a response to injury). This is a fine but important distinction for Nietzsche.

## Why It Matters

Without §11, the essay could be misread as claiming that morality is *entirely* reactive — that guilt, conscience, and justice are all just sublimated resentment. Nietzsche needs to show that the active, form-giving, power-exercising forces are *primary*, and reactive feelings are secondary. This protects his entire genealogical project from collapsing into the very ressentiment-theory he criticizes.

## Discussion Angles

- Does the distinction between active and reactive feelings hold up? When you feel wronged and seek justice, is that reactive (ressentiment) or active (exercising power)?
- Is Nietzsche consistent? He attributes moral creation to both active forces (justice, §11) and reactive mechanisms (bad conscience, §16). Are these compatible?
- The First Essay argued slave morality comes from ressentiment. The Second Essay argues bad conscience comes from internalization. How do these two origin stories relate?
