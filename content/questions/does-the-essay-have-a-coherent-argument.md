# Does the Essay Have a Coherent Argument?

## The Question

The Second Essay jumps from: the problem of promising (§1–3) → the creditor-debtor relationship (§4–8) → justice and ressentiment (§9–11) → a methodological excursus on form vs. meaning (§12–13) → the failure of punishment (§14–15) → bad conscience and the state (§16–18) → guilt before God (§19–22) → the need for a counter-ideal (§23–25). Is there a single argumentative thread running through all 25 sections, or is the essay a deliberately fragmentary collection of related genealogical investigations?

## The Case for Coherence

One way to reconstruct the argument as a single thread:

1. **The problem**: How did humans become animals that can make promises? (§1)
2. **The external solution**: Through the morality of mores — violent memory-making, social coercion (§2–3)
3. **The economic substrate**: The creditor-debtor relationship provides the conceptual vocabulary for guilt, duty, obligation (§4–8)
4. **The communal scale**: This logic scales up to communities, justice, and law (§9–11)
5. **The methodological warning**: But origin ≠ purpose; don't confuse where something came from with what it means now (§12–13)
6. **The failure**: Punishment — the obvious enforcement mechanism — doesn't actually produce guilt-feelings (§14–15)
7. **The real mechanism**: Bad conscience — the internalization of instincts — is what produces guilt as lived experience (§16–18)
8. **The religious escalation**: Bad conscience seizes on the God-hypothesis to reach maximum intensity (§19–22)
9. **The way out**: Bad conscience might be redirectable; a "man of the future" might overcome the guilt-apparatus (§23–25)

On this reading, the essay *does* have a coherent argument: it traces the genealogy of guilt from its economic origins through its psychological mechanism to its religious apex, then hints at its overcoming.

## The Case for Fragmentation

- The transitions between topics are often abrupt. Nietzsche himself uses phrases like "to return to our subject" (§8) and "but what am I saying?" (§25), suggesting even he recognizes digressions
- §11's attack on Dühring and §12's methodological excursus feel like interruptions (see [[questions/why-attack-ressentiment-here]])
- Nietzsche's own [[form-vs-meaning]] principle (§12) suggests that an argument can be "a continuous sign-chain of ever new interpretations" with no linear *progressus*. Perhaps the essay deliberately practices what it preaches
- The essay addresses at least four distinct phenomena — promising, guilt, punishment, bad conscience — that are related but not identical. The connections between them may be associative rather than logical

## A Middle Position

Perhaps the essay is **thematically coherent but rhetorically non-linear**. The theme (the genealogy of guilt/bad conscience) is consistent throughout, but Nietzsche explores it by circling, digressing, interrupting himself, and approaching from unexpected angles. This mirrors the [[form-vs-meaning]] insight: the "meaning" of the essay (what it's about) is stable, but the "form" (how it proceeds) is fluid and improvised.

The essay's structure might itself be an instance of will to power — Nietzsche imposing new interpretations on old material, rather than building a deductive argument step by step.

## Why It Matters

How you read the essay's structure affects how you read its claims:
- If it's coherent, then each section *builds on* the previous ones, and the §23–25 conclusion follows from the entire argument
- If it's fragmentary, then each section stands somewhat independently, and the connections between them are suggestive rather than necessary
- If it's thematically unified but formally free, then the essay is modeling a *way of thinking* — genealogical, associative, improvisatory — not just presenting a conclusion

## Leiter's Answer: Genealogical Method as the Unifying Structure

Leiter (Ch. 5, Ch. 7) argues the essay is coherent because it follows [[genealogy-as-method|genealogical method]] — a systematic procedure with identifiable principles. The essay is not a linear argument but a genealogical investigation, and its seemingly disparate sections each serve a specific methodological function:

- §1-3 provide the **naturalistic explanation** for calculability
- §4-8 identify **who benefits** from the guilt concept (the creditor)
- §12-13 establish the **form/meaning separation** that governs the analysis
- §14-15 **undermine MPS's self-understanding** (punishment doesn't produce guilt)
- §16-18 provide the **alternative mechanism** (internalization)
- §23 demonstrates **contingency** (things could have been otherwise)

On this reading, the essay has a coherent argument — it's just not a linear one. It is a systematic application of genealogical method to the concept of guilt, and each "digression" serves a purpose within that method.

## Discussion Angles

- Try to state the essay's "main argument" in one sentence. Can you? What gets lost?
- Which section feels most like a digression? Could the essay work without it?
- Does the essay's non-linear structure enhance or hinder your understanding?
- Compare this essay's structure to the First Essay (master/slave morality). Is one more coherent than the other?
- Does Leiter's genealogical-method reading make the essay feel more unified? Or does it impose a systematic structure that Nietzsche would resist?
