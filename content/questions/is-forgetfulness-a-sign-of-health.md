# Is Forgetfulness a Sign of Health?

## The Question

Nietzsche says forgetting is a sign of robust health — that the person who cannot forget is like a dyspeptic, unable to finish with anything. Do you agree? Is there something you think you *should* have forgotten by now but haven't — and has holding onto it served you or cost you?

## What Nietzsche Claims

Forgetfulness is not passive failure but an "active and in the strictest sense positive faculty of repression" (§1). It is a doorkeeper, a preserver of "psychic order, repose, and etiquette." Without it, "no happiness, no cheerfulness, no hope, no pride, no *present*." The person who cannot forget is like a dyspeptic — unable to digest experience, unable to be done with anything.

The entire Western philosophical and moral tradition treats memory as the precondition of selfhood, responsibility, and virtue — you can't be held accountable if you don't remember, you can't develop character without continuity. Nietzsche flips this: forgetfulness isn't a failure, it's robust health. Memory of the will is the *exception*, forced into existence against the grain of our nature.

He uses the metaphor of **digestion** throughout §1 — "inpsychation," "incorporation," the dyspeptic who can't finish with anything. Nietzsche is deliberately using biological and physiological language for what we normally treat as purely mental or moral processes. He's naturalizing conscience from the very first paragraph.

## The Tension

If forgetfulness is health, then the entire project of making man calculable — the [[memory-and-promising|memory of the will]], mnemotechnics, the morality of mores — is a *violation* of health. The capacity to promise is an achievement, but it's also an injury. The [[sovereign-individual]] has earned the right to make promises, but they've done so by overcoming something that was *good* (active forgetfulness) with something that was *forced* (memory).

Does the sovereign individual restore a *higher* form of forgetfulness — the ability to choose what to hold and what to release? Or is sovereignty itself a wound that can never fully heal?

## Discussion Angles

- Think of a grudge, a regret, or a commitment you can't stop thinking about. Has it made you *deeper* or just more burdened?
- Is the modern inability to disconnect — from phones, news, social media — a failure of the faculty of forgetfulness?
- Does therapy aim to help people *remember* (Freud) or to help them *forget* (move on, let go, accept)?
- Is Nietzsche's rehabilitation of forgetfulness compatible with trauma theory, which says suppressed memories are pathological?
