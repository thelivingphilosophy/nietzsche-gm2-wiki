# What Does Nietzsche Want Us to Do with Guilt?

## The Question

The essay traces guilt from its origins in debt (§4), through communal punishment (§9), bad conscience (§16), and religious self-torture (§22), to a hint of future redemption (§24). But what is the *prescription*? Does Nietzsche want us to overcome guilt, redirect it, historicize it into irrelevance, or something else entirely?

## Possible Readings

### 1. Abolish Guilt (the Liberation Reading)

Nietzsche traces guilt to contingent historical causes (debt, state formation, religion) — not to human nature or moral truth. If guilt is an artifact of specific power arrangements, we could in principle free ourselves from it.

**Evidence:**
- §20 suggests that declining belief in God could bring a "second innocence"
- The genealogical method itself demystifies guilt — showing its origins strips it of inevitability
- Nietzsche consistently opposes the "will to nothingness" that guilt feeds

**Problem:** Bad conscience created human interiority (§16). If you abolish guilt, do you also abolish the soul?

### 2. Redirect Guilt (the Self-Overcoming Reading)

Don't eliminate bad conscience — turn it against different targets. Instead of feeling guilty about your natural instincts (as Christianity demands), feel guilty about your *failure to affirm life*.

**Evidence:**
- §24 calls for bad conscience to be "wed" to "the unnatural inclinations" — all ideals hitherto that are "hostile to life and ideals that slander the world"
- §24 envisions a "man of the future" who redeems humanity not by eliminating guilt but by pointing it in a new direction
- §23 shows this was possible once before: the Greek gods took on "not the punishment but, what is nobler, the guilt" — preserving human dignity rather than destroying it
- Nietzsche's concept of self-overcoming (*Selbstaufhebung*) suggests transformation, not abolition

**Problem:** This still uses guilt as a motivational mechanism. Is redirected guilt any healthier than religious guilt?

### 3. Understand Guilt (the Genealogical Reading)

The essay's primary purpose is *diagnostic*, not prescriptive. By understanding where guilt comes from, we gain critical distance — we're no longer trapped inside it. The genealogy is itself the therapy.

**Evidence:**
- The essay is called *Zur Genealogie der Moral* — it's an investigation, not a manifesto
- §25 breaks off explicitly: "But what am I saying? Enough! Enough!" — the prescription is deferred
- Nietzsche elsewhere (in *Ecce Homo*) describes the *Genealogy* as preparatory — it clears the ground for *Zarathustra*'s positive vision

**Problem:** Diagnosis without prescription risks leaving us in the "great nausea" (§24) — knowing the disease without offering a cure.

### 4. Affirm the Whole History (the Amor Fati Reading)

Perhaps Nietzsche doesn't want us to do anything *with* guilt. The genealogy reveals guilt as part of the messy, terrible, creative process of human becoming. To wish guilt away would be to wish away everything it produced — interiority, ideals, the capacity for self-mastery. The task is to *affirm* the whole process, not escape it.

**Evidence:**
- Bad conscience is both illness and pregnancy (§18)
- Nietzsche's broader theme of self-overcoming (*Selbstaufhebung*) suggests great moral systems destroy themselves from within — guilt's self-overcoming is part of its greatness
- Nietzsche's broader philosophy of *amor fati* (love of fate) suggests affirming *everything* that has happened

**Problem:** This can feel like a sophisticated way of saying "do nothing" — if everything is affirmed, what distinguishes good from bad?

## Why It Matters

This is the practical question for book club: after reading 25 sections on the genealogy of guilt, what are we supposed to *do* differently? The fact that the essay doesn't give a clean answer is itself significant — Nietzsche may believe that premature answers are more dangerous than open questions.

## Scholarly Perspectives

**Leiter (2014):** The genealogy doesn't tell us what to *do* with guilt — it shows guilt has a complete natural explanation and thereby removes its authority as a self-evident moral reality. The practical consequence is anti-realism: guilt isn't *wrong*, but there's no reason to think it tracks objective moral facts. What you do with that insight depends on your type-facts. See [[naturalism-and-mps]].

**Janaway (2006):** The genealogy is therapeutic preparation for the creation of new values. Understanding guilt's genealogy "brings with it a *feeling* of diminution in value" — it loosens guilt's emotional grip, creating space for a future revaluation. But the new values themselves cannot be prescribed by the philosopher; they must be discovered by each individual from within their own character. This is the project of §24's "man of the future." See [[connections/autonomy-and-new-values]].

## Discussion Angles

- Which of these four readings feels most convincing to you? Why?
- Think about a specific guilt you carry. How does each reading change your relationship to it?
- Is there a fifth option — something Nietzsche hints at but doesn't develop?
- If genealogical understanding is itself the therapy, is it enough? Does knowing where guilt comes from change how it *feels*?
- Janaway says the point is not to eliminate guilt but to create space for *new* values. What would those values look like? Can you imagine a non-guilty relationship to your own instincts?
