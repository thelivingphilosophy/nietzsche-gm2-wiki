# Does Genealogy Debunk Morality?

## The Question

The Second Essay shows that guilt originated in debt, conscience in pain, and bad conscience in state violence. Does this genealogy *debunk* guilt — show it to be illegitimate? Or does it merely explain how guilt arose, leaving its validity untouched?

## The Genetic Fallacy Problem

The simplest reading: Nietzsche shows guilt has a shameful origin (debt, cruelty, power), therefore guilt is bad. But this is the genetic fallacy — inferring from the origin of a belief to its truth or falsity. Nietzsche knows this: §12 explicitly warns against confusing origin and current function. "The cause of the origin of a thing and its eventual utility, its actual employment and place in a system of purposes, lie worlds apart."

If Nietzsche is not committing the genetic fallacy, what argumentative work does genealogy do?

## Three Scholarly Answers

### Leiter: Explanatory Undermining

Leiter (2014: 120, 133-146) argues genealogy is an *explanatory* project. By providing a complete naturalistic explanation for why people hold moral beliefs — in terms of type-facts, psychological drives, and historical circumstances — Nietzsche removes the *need* to posit objective moral facts. If we can explain everything about guilt without assuming guilt tracks moral reality, then parsimony favors the anti-realist position.

This is not the genetic fallacy because Nietzsche is not arguing "guilt has a bad origin, therefore guilt is unjustified." He is arguing "guilt has a *complete natural explanation*, therefore we don't need to think it corresponds to moral facts."

### Janaway: Affective Loosening

Janaway (2006: 353-354) argues genealogy works on the reader's *affects*, not their beliefs. Discovering that guilt originated in debt-relationships and self-cruelty "brings with it a *feeling* of diminution in value" — it makes the emotional grip of guilt weaker, even if it doesn't logically refute guilt's claims. Genealogy is therapeutic: it creates the psychological space for a "revaluation" by loosening automatic allegiance to inherited values.

Nietzsche writes: "Your insight into how such things as moral judgements could ever have come into existence would spoil these emotional words for you" (GS 335). The insight doesn't *refute* the words — it *spoils* them. It undermines their emotional hold.

### The Undermining-of-Self-Understanding Reading

A third approach (synthesizing Leiter and Janaway): genealogy doesn't debunk morality directly but undermines MPS's *self-understanding*. MPS presents itself as based on free will, rational insight, and universal applicability. Genealogy shows that each of these is a fiction produced by identifiable psychological and historical processes. The debunking is not of morality itself but of morality's *self-image*.

In the Second Essay: MPS says "guilt is the voice of conscience, an innate sense of right and wrong." The genealogy responds: "No — guilt is a compound of debt-logic, internalized cruelty, and religious reinterpretation. You *call* it the voice of conscience, but its actual causal history has nothing to do with moral insight." This doesn't prove guilt is wrong, but it strips it of its claim to be self-evidently right.

## Why This Matters for Book Club

How you answer this question determines how you read the entire essay:

- **If genealogy debunks:** The essay is an attack on guilt. Nietzsche wants us to stop feeling guilty, and the genealogy provides the intellectual grounds for doing so.
- **If genealogy merely explains:** The essay is a scientific project — understanding where guilt comes from, without prescribing what to do about it.
- **If genealogy loosens affects:** The essay is therapeutic preparation for a future revaluation — clearing the ground for new values by weakening the emotional grip of old ones.

## Discussion Angles

- Does knowing guilt's genealogy actually change how you *feel* about guilt? Or is guilt immune to intellectual genealogy?
- Is there a difference between debunking *this* guilt (Christian, theological, infinite-debt guilt) and debunking guilt *as such*?
- The Greeks had guilt without Christianity (§23). Does their example suggest a "healthy" guilt is possible — one not rooted in the ascetic ideal?
- If genealogy only works on those with the right type-facts (Leiter's point), what does it do for readers who *don't* have "ears related to ours"?
