# Two Kinds of Free Will: Deserts vs. Agency

## The Question

Nietzsche appears to contradict himself about free will. In GM I:13, he ridicules the idea that there is a "doer behind the deed" — no neutral subject choosing to act. Yet in GM II:2, he celebrates the sovereign individual as "autonomous," a "master of a free will." How can he deny free will and affirm it in the same book?

## Gemes's Resolution: Two Concepts, Not One

Ken Gemes (in Gemes & Janaway, 2006) argues the contradiction dissolves once we distinguish two kinds of free will:

**Deserts free will** — the metaphysical notion that agents *could have done otherwise*, which grounds moral responsibility, punishment, and reward. This is the "superlative, metaphysical sense" of free will (BGE 21) that Nietzsche calls an "error" and a "fable" throughout his career. Deserts free will requires a neutral "doer behind the deed" — the very thing GM I:13 attacks.

**Agency free will** — the naturalistic notion that some individuals achieve *genuine selfhood*: a unified, integrated hierarchy of drives constituting a real character, a person who acts from who they are rather than being tossed by competing impulses. This is the sovereign individual's "free will" — not freedom from causation but freedom from internal fragmentation.

On Gemes's reading, Nietzsche **rejects** deserts free will and **affirms** agency free will. The negative passages (HAH I:39, BGE 21, GM I:13) attack the metaphysical fiction of uncaused choice. The positive passages (GM II:2, the Goethe portrait in TI) celebrate the rare achievement of genuine agency.

## Leiter's Counter-Reading

Brian Leiter (2002/2014) reads Nietzsche as a thoroughgoing **fatalist**: type-facts about persons are causally primary, the "voluntary is absolutely lacking" (WP 458), and the sovereign individual passage is either ironic or describes a figure whose "autonomy" is itself the product of type-facts rather than genuine self-creation.

For Leiter, there is no positive Nietzschean concept of free will. What looks like agency free will is just the expression of favorable type-facts — Goethe was Goethe because of who he *was*, not because he *chose* to become Goethe. The fatalist reading means the sovereign individual's "mastery over fate" is not self-creation but the lucky alignment of character and circumstance.

## Janaway's Middle Path

Christopher Janaway (in the same 2006 paper) traces a **development** in Nietzsche's thinking. In the early *Human, All Too Human*, Nietzsche follows Schopenhauer and Ree in flatly denying free will. But by the time of the *Genealogy*, his interest has shifted: he no longer argues against free will as a metaphysical thesis but instead asks what **affective states** drive people to believe in it.

Janaway argues:
- GM I:13's attack on the "doer behind the deed" is not a general denial of agency but a **genealogical explanation** of why the weak *need* to believe in the neutral subject — it lets them hold the strong *accountable* for being strong
- The sovereign individual represents a **positive reconception** of freedom: acting fully within one's character, knowing its limits, and embracing one's actions as genuinely one's own
- Free will in this sense is a **rare achievement**, not a universal endowment — possible only for those with a "stable, unified, and integrated hierarchy of drives" (Gemes 2006: 327)

## Why This Matters for Essay 2

The deserts-vs-agency distinction illuminates several moments in the Second Essay:

- **§1-3**: The "breeding" of memory and promising is the construction of a capacity for **agency** — not the metaphysical endowment of free choice. Most humans acquire only the capacity for conformist promising (Stage 2); genuine agency (Stage 3, the sovereign individual) requires something more
- **§13 (GM I:13 echo)**: Nietzsche's attack on punishment as retribution (it presupposes deserts free will) is compatible with his celebration of the sovereign individual (who has agency free will)
- **§14-15**: Punishment fails to produce guilt precisely because it addresses the *deed* rather than the *agent* — it presupposes deserts free will when what matters is the constitution of the person
- **§16-18**: Bad conscience creates the *interiority* necessary for genuine selfhood. If agency free will requires self-knowledge and internal organization, then bad conscience — painful as it is — is a precondition for sovereignty
- **§24**: The "man of the future" who redirects bad conscience must have genuine agency — he must be capable of *choosing* which direction to turn the will to power. But this "choice" is not deserts free will; it is the expression of a strong, unified character

## Discussion Angles

- Does the distinction between deserts and agency free will actually solve the contradiction, or does it just rename it?
- If agency free will is a rare achievement, what does this imply about moral responsibility for ordinary people? Is Nietzsche denying that most humans are genuine agents?
- Gemes argues that most moderns are "merely passive conduits for various disparate forces" (2006: 332) — not genuine persons. Is this a descriptive claim or a normative one?
- How does Gemes's reading of the sovereign individual as a genuine Nietzschean ideal square with the sovereign individual's near-total absence from the rest of the essay?
