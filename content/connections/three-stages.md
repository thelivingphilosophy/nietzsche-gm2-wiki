# The Three Stages

A developmental framework that emerges from reading the Second Essay as a whole. Nietzsche tells a story with three stages, and much of the confusion about the essay comes from conflating them. These stages are not strictly chronological — they are **psychological strata** that can coexist in the same individual, culture, or historical moment.

## Stage 1: The Pre-Social Animal (The [[blond-beast|Blond Beast]])

Pure instinct, active [[memory-and-promising|forgetfulness]], full outward discharge. The blond beast lives in the present moment — magnificent vitality but no depth, no interiority, no capacity for promises. Freedom without reflection.

Key traits:
- Cruelty discharged outward, experienced as innocent and festive
- No memory of the will — lives in present affect
- No self-reflection — inner world "as thin as if stretched between two membranes" (§16)
- Operates as a *pack* (natural hierarchy) not a *mob* (reactive herd)

The blond beast is **not** the sovereign individual. It lacks the interiority, self-mastery, and memory of the will that sovereignty requires. It is the *raw material*, not the finished product.

**Important:** The blond beast and the mob/herd animal are both present in Stage 1 but are different. The blond beast's affect is active, expansive, outward-moving. The mob/herd animal's affect is reactive, contracting, defensive. This maps onto the First Essay's active/reactive distinction. They are not racial or cultural types but **poles of a psychological spectrum present in every individual** — as Solzhenitsyn puts it, "the line of good and evil runs through the heart of every man."

## Stage 2: The Social Straitjacket (The Moral Animal)

The city walls go up. Outward discharge is blocked. Instincts are forced inward. The "morality of mores" (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*) makes man calculable, regular, predictable through punishment, custom, and social pressure.

Key traits:
- Instinct suppressed, turned inward → origin of [[bad-conscience]]
- Memory of the will imposed from outside through pain and fear (see [[memory-and-promising|mnemotechnics]])
- Regularity is heteronomous — comes from external constraint, not self-mastery
- Promises are backed by the *system* (punishment, social shame), not by the self
- Interiority develops — the soul gains depth — but as a wound, not an achievement

This stage is itself double: the **noble** version retains more outward vitality (periodically discharging through war, hunting — going "outside the walls"), while the **slave** version produces guilt, ressentiment, and moral self-condemnation. Same compression, different outcomes depending on the ratio of active to reactive tendency in the individual.

### The Paradox of Homogenization

Stage 2 is explicitly a **homogenizing** process — making everyone "uniform, like among like, regular, and consequently calculable" (§2). Yet it produces, as its "ripest fruit," the radically *singular* [[sovereign-individual]]. How?

The sovereign individual is not the *average* product but the **rarest exception**. The tree of social discipline produces mostly ordinary calculable members of the herd. But occasionally, under the right conditions, it produces someone who has internalized the discipline so completely that they no longer need its external enforcement. The scaffold gets kicked away once the building can stand on its own.

You cannot have genuine self-mastery without having first had something to master. The sovereign individual is not produced *despite* homogenization but *through* it.

## Stage 3: The Sovereign Individual

The ripe fruit. This person has passed through Stage 2 and come out the other side: autonomous, supramoral, possessing a genuine will consistent across time. See [[sovereign-individual]] for full treatment.

Key traits:
- Instinct integrated — directed by self, neither suppressed nor unreflective
- Promises rarely, slowly, with full weight — word is bond, backed by self alone
- Regularity flows from internal constitution, not external enforcement
- Conscience is *proud self-awareness*, not guilt
- Has both the vitality of Stage 1 *and* the depth of Stage 2, but integrated

Note: the sovereign individual column is thin on historical exemplars. Nietzsche can barely name anyone who fully embodies it — it functions more as a regulative ideal than a description of actual existing humans.

## Mob Instinct Across the Stages

The mob instinct is present in *both* Stage 1 and Stage 2 but means something different in each.

**In Stage 1:** Raw collective affect — the herd behaviour of pre-social groups, impulsive, reactive, running on immediate feeling. It manifests as external violence, raiding, sudden brutality. Unreflective, momentary, discharged outward. The passage about the Germans employing "fearful means to master their basic mob-instinct and its brutal coarseness" (§3) describes the *transition moment* — Stage 1 mob instinct being forcibly suppressed by Stage 2 mechanisms. The cruelty of the punishment is proportional to the strength of the mob instinct it's trying to overcome.

**In Stage 2:** The mob instinct doesn't disappear — it gets *driven underground*. Once the city walls go up and outward discharge is blocked, the same collective reactive energy becomes the substrate for herd morality, ressentiment, and slave morality. It transforms from *brutal coarseness* venting outward into something more insidious — collective moral pressure, the tyranny of custom, the instinct to level and equalise. In Nietzsche's view, Stage 2 mob instinct is actually *more* dangerous because it has been given moral clothing. Raw Stage 1 brutality is at least honest. Stage 2 mob instinct disguises itself as justice, fairness, equality.

**Important distinction:** The blond beast and the mob/herd animal are both present in Stage 1 but occupy opposite poles. The blond beast operates as a *pack* (natural hierarchy, active affect) while the mob operates as a *herd* (reactive, defensive clustering). They coexist within the same social formations, and even within the same individuals — the question is which tendency dominates.

## The Stages Are Not Strictly Historical

Nietzsche writes in §9: "this prehistory is in any case present in all ages or may always reappear." Like Jung's collective unconscious, the archaic layer isn't *past* — it's *deep*. Stage 1 psychology persists underneath Stage 2 social formation and can always resurface.

Population density and social complexity shift the **balance** between stages rather than replacing one with another. Even in warrior cultures, both the blond beast and the herd animal coexist; the city walls change not the *existence* of the tension but the *balance of power* within it.

The *intensity* of Stage 2 compression is a function of social complexity and density rather than a binary switch. In a small tribal warrior culture you have some suppression of individual instinct for collective function, but also regular legitimate discharge through warfare and hunting — the ratio of compression to discharge is manageable. As population density increases, opportunities for legitimate discharge diminish relative to the pressure being generated. This is why Nietzsche's examples of extreme mnemotechnics belong to relatively recent history rather than deep prehistory. The more complex the society, the more violent the methods needed to enforce the memory of the will.

Scholars generally treat the chronology as *mythological* rather than historical in any precise sense. Bernard Williams notes that Nietzsche's genealogical method is closer to "the story of how things might have gone" — a plausible psychological reconstruction rather than a historical claim. Brian Leiter similarly argues the genealogical narrative is better read as a hypothesis about psychological mechanisms than as a claim about datable events. The city walls moment is vaguely associated with the earliest settled civilisations (~3000–4000 BCE), but Nietzsche never says this explicitly.

This is what makes Nietzsche's diagnosis personal rather than merely anthropological: the question is not "which historical stage are we in?" but "which tendency dominates in *me*?"

## Key Quotes

> "The ripest fruit is the *sovereign individual*, like only to himself, liberated again from morality of custom, autonomous and supramoral." (§2)

> "All instincts that do not discharge themselves outwardly *turn inward* — this is what I call the *internalization* of man." (§16)

> "This prehistory is in any case present in all ages or may always reappear." (§9)

## Scholarly Commentary

### Leiter: Types Respond Differently to Each Stage

Leiter's [[scholars/doctrine-of-types|doctrine of types]] implies that different types respond differently to Stage 2 compression. The blond beast type — organized for external conquest — suffers the most violent internalization because their drive to form-giving is strongest. Weaker, more adaptable types may adjust more easily, producing conformist calculability rather than the creative pathology of bad conscience. This is why the same historical process produces herd animals *and* (potentially) sovereign individuals: different type-facts under the same conditions yield different outcomes.

### Gemes: The Transition to Stage 3 Is Not Guaranteed

Gemes (2006: 327-332) emphasizes that the transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3 is not a natural development — it is a [[scholars/personhood-as-achievement|rare achievement]] that most humans never accomplish. Stage 2 produces interiority (bad conscience), but interiority alone doesn't produce genuine selfhood. You also need the hierarchical ordering of drives that constitutes a real "I." Most humans remain in Stage 2 indefinitely — not because they lack interiority but because they lack the capacity (or the luck) to organize it.
