# Cruelty Sublimated, Not Eliminated

One of the essay's most persistent through-lines: cruelty does not disappear as civilization advances. It changes its *form*, its *direction*, and its *name* — but the underlying instinct persists. Tracing this thread across the essay reveals Nietzsche's deepest claim about human nature: the drive to inflict suffering is ineradicable, and every moral and religious system is powered by some version of it.

## The Three Phases

### Phase 1: External Cruelty (§5–7)

In the archaic world, cruelty is **outward, public, and festive**. The creditor inflicts suffering on the debtor's body as payment. Executions, tortures, and punishments are communal spectacles — "festivals" that provide genuine pleasure to participants and onlookers alike.

"Without cruelty there is no festival" (§7). Cruelty at this stage is not hidden or shameful. It is a *normal quality of man* to which "conscience cordially says *Yes!*" (§6).

### Phase 2: Internalized Cruelty (§16–18)

When the instincts are blocked from outward discharge (by the formation of the state, §17), cruelty turns **inward**. "All instincts that do not discharge themselves outwardly *turn inward*" (§16). Man begins to persecute, attack, and torment *himself*.

The external festival becomes an internal one: "this secret self-ravishment, this artists' cruelty, this delight in imposing a form upon oneself as a hard, recalcitrant, suffering material" (§18). The same drive that once enjoyed public torture now takes "dreadfully joyous" pleasure in self-torture.

### Phase 3: Religious Self-Torture (§22)

Bad conscience seizes on the **religious hypothesis** to achieve its most extreme expression. The concept of God provides the ultimate framework for self-punishment. Man "stretches himself upon the contradiction 'God' and 'Devil'" and projects "all his denial of himself, of his nature, naturalness, and actuality, in the form of an affirmation, as something existent, corporeal, real, as God, as the holiness of God, as God the Judge, as God the Hangman, as the beyond, as eternity, as torment without end, as hell, as the immeasurability of punishment and guilt" (§22).

The emphasis on *will* is crucial: this is "the *will* of man to find himself guilty and reprehensible to a degree that can never be atoned for; his *will* to think himself punished without any possibility of the punishment becoming equal to the guilt" (§22). Religious self-torture is not passive suffering — it is *active* self-destruction, the will to power turned against its own possessor with maximum force.

Here cruelty reaches its apex: man tortures himself *on behalf of the divine*, interpreting his own nature as something that deserves punishment. The "festival" has been completely internalized and sanctified.

## The Continuity

What connects these three phases is the **same instinct** operating under different conditions:

| Phase | Direction | Object | Name |
|-------|-----------|--------|------|
| Archaic | Outward | Others' bodies | Festival, punishment |
| Civilized | Inward | One's own psyche | Bad conscience |
| Religious | Inward + upward | One's own nature before God | Sin, guilt, unworthiness |

Nietzsche's point is not that civilized humans are *less* cruel than their ancestors. They are cruel in different ways — ways that are harder to recognize because they wear "innocent names" (§7) and operate through interiority rather than spectacle.

## Why This Matters

This through-line carries several implications:

1. **Moral progress is not the elimination of cruelty** — it's the redirection of cruelty into forms that modern sensibility can tolerate (or fail to recognize)
2. **Religious morality is not the *opposite* of barbaric cruelty** — it's the same instinct operating at a higher level of refinement
3. **Any future morality must deal with this instinct** — it cannot be wished away or moralized out of existence. The question is not "how do we eliminate cruelty?" but "what do we do with the ineradicable drive to inflict suffering?"

This is one reason Nietzsche looks to the [[ascetic-ideals-foreshadowed|possibility of redirecting bad conscience]] (§23–24) rather than simply abolishing it.

## Discussion Angles

- Is Nietzsche right that cruelty is ineradicable, or is this a projection from 19th-century assumptions?
- Can you identify modern forms of "sublimated cruelty" that fit Nietzsche's pattern (social media shaming, cancel culture, self-optimization culture)?
- If cruelty can only be redirected, not eliminated, what's the *best* direction to point it in?
