# Two Threads: City Walls and Creditor-Debtor

The Second Essay runs two distinct genealogical threads simultaneously, and much of its complexity comes from the fact that Nietzsche doesn't always clearly signal which thread he's pulling on. Understanding their relationship is key to understanding the essay's architecture.

## Thread 1: The City Walls / Internalization Thread

**Sections:** primarily §16–18 (but set up by §1–3)

The story of what happens to instinct under social compression. Outward discharge gets blocked, cruelty turns inward, interiority develops, [[bad-conscience]] emerges as a raw psychological wound. This is almost **thermodynamic** — energy that can't go out must go in. The soul gets depth because the pressure has nowhere else to go.

This thread is about the **formation of the inner world** and is fundamentally *pre-moral* — it happens before guilt in the moralized sense exists at all.

Operates at the level of **individual psychology** — what happens inside a person when instinct is suppressed. It doesn't require a specific social relationship, just the general fact of social enclosure.

## Thread 2: The Creditor-Debtor / Economic Thread

**Sections:** primarily §4–8

The story of how economic relationships generate moral concepts. Debt creates obligation, obligation creates memory of the will, failure to repay creates the right to extract pain, and gradually the whole apparatus of guilt, conscience, duty, and responsibility crystallizes out of commercial interactions. This thread is almost **juridical**.

Operates at the level of **interpersonal and social structure** — it requires at minimum two people in a specific kind of relationship. Nietzsche says in §8 that the [[creditor-debtor]] relationship is where "one person first *measured himself* against another."

## What Each Thread Produces

| Thread | Produces | Level |
|--------|----------|-------|
| City walls / internalization | Bad conscience as raw painful interiority — cruelty turned inward | Individual psychology |
| Creditor-debtor | The *concepts* of guilt, obligation, duty, justice — the moral *vocabulary* | Social/conceptual structure |

**The crucial connection:** the creditor-debtor logic provides the *conceptual framework* through which the raw wound of bad conscience gets *moralized*.

- Without the internalization thread → no psychological raw material, no inward pain, no inner life capable of sustaining guilt
- Without the creditor-debtor thread → no conceptual vocabulary to interpret that pain as *moral* rather than merely physical

## Mutually Constitutive

The most compelling reading is that the threads are not just intertwined but **mutually constitutive** — each making the other possible:

**City walls enable debt:** The creditor-debtor relationship requires a certain level of internalization to function — you need people who can hold obligations across time, who have enough inner life to feel the weight of a debt. A pure blond beast with no interiority can't really be a debtor in the morally significant sense.

**Debt shapes internalization:** The internalization process is partly *driven by* creditor-debtor logic — the memory of the will that gets burned in through [[memory-and-promising|mnemotechnics]] is specifically memory of *obligations*, of debts, of promises made in contractual relationships. The content of what gets inscribed through punishment is largely economic and juridical.

**The synthesis:** The city walls create the psychological space in which debt can become obligation, and the creditor-debtor relationship provides the specific content that gets burned into that psychological space through punishment.

## The Cleanest Formulation

> The city walls thread explains **how** humans got an inner life.
> The creditor-debtor thread explains **what that inner life got filled with**.

The result of both together is the moralized guilty conscience — a creature with enough interiority to suffer from itself, furnished with a conceptual vocabulary that ensures it will do exactly that.

## Scholarly Divergence

This structural question connects to the Ridley vs Risse debate about [[guilt-before-god]]:

- **Ridley's reading** implicitly prioritizes the city walls thread — internalization produces bad conscience first, the creditor-debtor vocabulary then moralizes it. Psychology is primary, economics is secondary.
- **Risse's reading** gives more weight to the creditor-debtor thread — the conceptual apparatus of debt and obligation is what makes guilt intelligible *as guilt* rather than just pain.

Nietzsche himself seems to lean toward Ridley's ordering — internalization happens first, creates the wound, and then the creditor-debtor logic is the tool that interprets and deepens that wound. But the essay's structure is genuinely ambiguous.

## Why This Matters

What we experience as a unified phenomenon — **moral guilt** — is actually the product of two completely separate genealogical processes that happened to converge. Guilt feels like one thing, natural and unified, but it's actually a historical accident: two independent threads that got woven together. This is part of what makes Nietzsche's diagnosis so deflationary and so unsettling.
