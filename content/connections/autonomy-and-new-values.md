# Autonomy and the Creation of New Values

Janaway (2006: 349-356) argues that the sovereign individual of §2 points not just toward self-mastery but toward the **creation of new values** — and that this distinguishes Nietzschean autonomy from Kantian autonomy.

## Kantian vs. Nietzschean Autonomy

Both Kant and Nietzsche describe a self-legislating individual who has internalized the law. But the difference is fundamental:

- **Kant's autonomous agent** legislates the moral law through pure practical reason. The law is universal, discovered through the categorical imperative, and the same for everyone. Autonomy is the capacity to *recognize* and *obey* a law that reason discloses.
- **Nietzsche's sovereign individual** is "liberated *again* from morality of custom, autonomous and supramoral" (§2). He has "his *own* measure of value" — not a universal law but a personal standard that arises from his own character. Autonomy is the capacity to *create* the framework within which one's actions have meaning.

The sovereign individual is explicitly **supramoral** — *beyond* morality, not just self-legislatingly *within* it. He doesn't give himself better rules; he creates the conditions under which rules (and values) emerge from his own character rather than from tradition, custom, or herd instinct.

## The Role of Bad Conscience

Janaway argues that this creative autonomy requires passing *through* bad conscience, not bypassing it. The morality of custom (§1-3) breeds the capacity for self-discipline. Bad conscience (§16-18) creates the interiority — the deep self-awareness — that makes genuine self-knowledge possible. Only someone with this depth of self-awareness can take the third step: discovering which values are genuinely their own, as opposed to inherited habits of feeling.

This is why §24's "man of the future" must redirect bad conscience rather than simply abolish it. The self-cruelty of bad conscience can be turned against the "unnatural inclinations" — the ascetic, life-denying values — rather than against natural instincts. This redirection is itself an act of value-creation: choosing what to affirm and what to attack, based on self-knowledge rather than inherited morality.

## Revaluation as Self-Discovery

Janaway emphasizes that Nietzsche's "revaluation of values" is not the imposition of a new universal code. It is a deeply personal process:

> "Rather than adhering to values which are received, traditional, generic, universal, one is to discover one's own personal values." (Janaway 2006: 352)

This requires what Nietzsche calls in *Daybreak* learning to "think differently" and ultimately to "feel differently" (D 103). The genealogical diagnosis — understanding that one's values are the product of historical psychological processes — creates a *suspension* of automatic allegiance to inherited values. From that suspension, genuine choice becomes possible — not the metaphysical "free choice" of deserts free will, but the psychological achievement of acting from a self-understood character.

Nietzsche writes: "Let us *limit* ourselves to the purification of our opinions and value judgements and to the *creation of tables of what is good that are new and all our own*" (GS 335). The emphasis on "new and all our own" signals that the values cannot be prescribed in advance by the philosopher — they must be discovered by the individual from within their own character.

## The Future Sovereign Individual

Janaway reads the sovereign individual of §2 not as a description of someone who already exists but as a **promissory note** — a portrait of what becomes possible once the morality of custom and its Christian intensification are left behind. The sovereign individual is the figure who:

1. Has passed through the morality of custom (§1-3) and acquired self-discipline
2. Has passed through bad conscience (§16-18) and acquired interiority
3. Has *not* succumbed to the moralization of bad conscience through Christianity (§19-22)
4. Has instead organized his drives into a unified self ([[personhood-as-achievement]])
5. Creates his own values from that self-knowledge

§24's "bell-stroke of noon and of the great decision that makes the will free *again*" suggests a future redemption pattern: there once were sovereign individuals, the morality of custom and Christianity destroyed the conditions for sovereignty, and the genealogical project clears the ground for sovereignty to become possible again.
