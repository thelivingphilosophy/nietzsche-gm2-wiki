# Everything Has a Price vs Beyond Justice

The essay begins by arguing that all moral concepts originate in the economic logic of debt, pricing, and equivalence: "everything has its price; *all* things can be paid for" (§8). But it also argues that the highest expression of justice is **mercy** — the powerful letting go of the debt entirely (§10). These two positions create a productive tension at the heart of the essay.

## The Price-Logic

The [[creditor-debtor]] relationship establishes that:
- Every injury has its *equivalent* and can be compensated (§4)
- The body can be collateral — flesh, freedom, life can pay for debts (§5)
- Even suffering can serve as payment, because making someone suffer provides pleasure to the creditor (§5–6)
- This price-logic is the "oldest and naivest moral canon of justice": "everything has its price; *all* things can be paid for" (§8)

This is the foundation of the essay's genealogy: morality begins in commerce, in the act of measuring one thing against another.

## The Transcendence of Price-Logic

But justice does not *stay* at the level of price:
- As a community grows powerful, it no longer needs strict equivalence — it can absorb injuries without retaliating (§10)
- "The creditor always becomes more humane to the extent that he has grown richer" — he can afford to let debts go (§10)
- Justice "ends by winking and letting those incapable of discharging their debt go free" (§10)
- This self-overcoming of justice receives "the beautiful name" of **mercy** (§10)

Mercy is described as "beyond the law" — the privilege of the most powerful, who no longer need the economy of compensation.

## The Tension

Can you trace the *origin* of justice to price-logic and also celebrate its *overcoming* of that logic? There are several ways to read this:

**1. Historical stages.** Price-logic came first historically; mercy emerged later as communities grew powerful. Nietzsche is simply describing a developmental sequence, not contradicting himself.

**2. The form-vs-meaning principle.** Following §12's method (see [[form-vs-meaning]]), the *origin* of justice (in price-logic) and its *current meaning* (which includes mercy) "lie worlds apart." Justice retained the form of the creditor-debtor relationship but transcended its original meaning.

**3. Power is the constant.** Both price-logic and mercy are expressions of **power**. The creditor who demands payment exercises power through enforcement; the merciful community exercises power through *the luxury of not enforcing*. The underlying principle isn't equivalence or forgiveness — it's the capacity to impose one's will.

**4. The tension is the point.** Perhaps Nietzsche means to show that justice is inherently unstable — always oscillating between the demand for payment and the capacity to forgive. Neither the strict economy of debt nor the generosity of mercy is justice's "true" nature; justice is the ongoing *negotiation* between them.

## Why This Matters

This tension tracks onto real moral questions:
- Is forgiveness a *transcendence* of the economy of debt, or a *luxury version* of it (available only to the powerful)?
- If "everything has its price," can anything be genuinely *given* (forgiven, donated, sacrificed) outside the logic of exchange?
- When Nietzsche reaches Christianity's "stroke of genius" in §21 — God paying himself for man's debt — is this mercy or the ultimate intensification of price-logic?

## Discussion Angles

- Does mercy require power? Can the weak forgive, or is forgiveness always a move the strong make?
- Think about modern debates about restorative vs. retributive justice. Where do they fall on the price-logic ↔ mercy spectrum?
- Is Nietzsche's genealogy of justice ultimately *for* or *against* the idea that everything has a price?
