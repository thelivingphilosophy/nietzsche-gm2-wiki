# Genealogy as Method

Leiter (*Nietzsche on Morality*, Ch. 5) and Janaway (2007) both clarify what Nietzsche is doing when he does "genealogy" — and what he is *not* doing. The Second Essay is the *Genealogy*'s most sustained application of the method, and understanding the method reframes the essay's structure.

## What Genealogy Is Not: The Genetic Fallacy

The most common misreading: showing that guilt originated in debt *debunks* guilt. This is the genetic fallacy — the inference from "X has an ignoble origin" to "X is illegitimate." Nietzsche explicitly rejects this reasoning in §12: "the cause of the origin of a thing and its eventual utility, its actual employment and place in a system of purposes, lie worlds apart." Origin does not determine current meaning. See [[form-vs-meaning]].

If genealogy is not the genetic fallacy, what argumentative work does it do?

## Leiter's Five Principles of Genealogy

Drawing on Ch. 5 (pp. 133-154) and the commentaries on each essay, Leiter identifies several principles that together constitute Nietzsche's genealogical method:

### 1. Naturalistic Explanation
Provide a *complete* natural explanation for why people hold the moral beliefs they do — in terms of psychology, physiology, and historical circumstance. If guilt can be fully explained by debt-relationships (§4-8), internalization of instincts (§16), and religious reinterpretation (§19-22), then there is no explanatory *need* for objective moral facts. The genealogy doesn't prove moral facts don't exist; it removes the *reason* to think they do.

In the Second Essay: the four layers of guilt (debt → community → feeling → theology) constitute a complete natural explanation of how guilt came to exist. No appeal to a moral law, a divine command, or an inherent sense of right and wrong is needed.

### 2. Type-Identification
Ask: *who benefits from this moral concept?* What kind of person created it, and what psychological needs does it serve? This is the "who is speaking?" question that Nietzsche identifies as fundamental to philological method.

In the Second Essay: the creditor-debtor relationship shows that guilt concepts serve the *creditor* — the powerful party who gets to extract suffering as compensation (§5-6). The moralization of guilt through religion serves the *ascetic priest* — who channels the self-cruelty of bad conscience into theological guilt, thereby gaining power over the suffering (GM III:15-20). See [[connections/three-essays-unified]].

### 3. Contingency Demonstration
Show that the current state of affairs was *not inevitable* — things could have gone otherwise. This undercuts the sense that our moral concepts are natural, necessary, or divinely ordained.

In the Second Essay: §23's Greek alternative is the clearest contingency demonstration. The Greeks had bad conscience too, but their gods *absorbed* guilt rather than intensifying it — "not the punishment but, what is nobler, the guilt" (§23). The Christian outcome (maximum guilt, infinite debt) was not the only possible outcome of bad conscience. It required specific historical conditions — the ascetic ideal — to produce. See [[connections/the-greek-alternative]].

### 4. Form vs. Meaning Separation
Separate the enduring *form* of a practice from the shifting *meanings* it has been given. Any practice that has persisted has been seized and reinterpreted by successive forces, so its current meaning tells you nothing about its origin, and vice versa (§12-13).

In the Second Essay: punishment's eleven-plus meanings (§13) are the showcase. But the principle applies everywhere: "conscience" means different things in §2 (proud self-mastery), §7 (affirmation of cruelty), §11 (the active type's clear eye), and §16 (bad conscience as self-cruelty). See [[form-vs-meaning]], [[conscience]].

### 5. Undermining Self-Understanding
Show that MPS's *self-description* is false. MPS presents itself as based on free will, transparency, and universal applicability. Genealogy shows these are fictions produced by specific historical and psychological processes, not descriptions of how things are.

In the Second Essay: the morality of mores (§1-3) reveals that promise-keeping is not a natural capacity but an artificial product of violent breeding. Free will is not a metaphysical given but a fiction that serves specific psychological needs (GM I:13, applied in II:14-15). The "moral consciousness" that MPS treats as self-evident is the product of bad conscience — a pathological internalization, not a rational insight.

## Janaway's Addition: Genealogy of Affects

Janaway (2007: 343-349) argues that the mature Nietzsche's genealogical method replaces earlier metaphysical arguments. Where the young Nietzsche (*Human, All Too Human*) argued directly that free will doesn't exist, the mature Nietzsche asks: what *affective states* — what feelings, drives, psychological needs — produce belief in free will? This is not a metaphysical argument but a psychological diagnosis.

In the Second Essay: Nietzsche doesn't argue that guilt is conceptually incoherent or metaphysically impossible. He traces the feelings that produce it (bad conscience), the concepts that shape it (debt), and the institutions that maximize it (Christianity). The genealogy is a history of affects, not a logical refutation.

## Genealogy and the Essay's Structure

Understanding genealogy as method explains why the Second Essay is structured the way it is:

- **§1-3** don't just describe memory and promising — they provide the *naturalistic explanation* for calculability (Principle 1)
- **§4-8** don't just note that *Schuld* means "debt" — they identify *who benefits* from the guilt-concept: the creditor (Principle 2)
- **§12-13** don't just make a methodological point — they establish the *form/meaning separation* that governs the rest of the essay (Principle 4)
- **§14-15** don't just say punishment fails — they *undermine MPS's self-understanding* by showing its key mechanism doesn't work (Principle 5)
- **§23** isn't just a nice contrast — it's a *contingency demonstration* that proves the Christian outcome wasn't necessary (Principle 3)

The essay is not a linear narrative but a systematic application of genealogical method to the concept of guilt.
