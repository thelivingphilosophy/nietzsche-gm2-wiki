# Schopenhauer and the Second Essay

Schopenhauer's influence on Nietzsche is pervasive but largely implicit in the Second Essay. Leiter (Ch. 2) and Janaway (2007) trace several threads that run from Schopenhauer's philosophy through the essay's central arguments.

## Agency, Character, and Fatalism

Schopenhauer denied free will in the phenomenal world: since the principle of sufficient reason governs all phenomena, "every individual action follows with strict necessity from the effect of the motive on the character" (Schopenhauer 1818: 113). We are "spectators" of our own actions — they may appear undetermined to us, but in reality they are completely determined by the interaction of motive and character (Schopenhauer 1818: 291).

Crucially, Schopenhauer held that each person has an "unalterable empirical character" — a fixed set of traits determining how they respond to motives. "These we can never change by influencing him, by instructing him" (1818: 294). Character is given, not chosen.

Nietzsche inherits this framework but strips away Schopenhauer's metaphysics (the noumenal will, intelligible character). What remains is a naturalistic fatalism: type-facts about persons are causally primary, character is relatively fixed, and "the voluntary is absolutely lacking" (WP 458). See [[scholars/doctrine-of-types]].

**In the Second Essay:** The morality of mores (§1-3) works on the body, not the mind — breeding calculability through pain. This is a Schopenhauerian picture: you cannot change character through rational instruction, only through physical intervention (mnemotechnics). The sovereign individual's "protracted will" (§2) is not a free choice but a character trait — something he *is*, not something he decided to become.

Janaway (2007: 340-343) argues that by the time of the *Genealogy*, Nietzsche has moved beyond Schopenhauer in a crucial respect: the early Nietzsche (HAH) simply denied free will following Schopenhauer and Ree, but the mature Nietzsche shifts to *genealogical* questioning — not "is free will real?" but "what psychological needs drive belief in free will?" This is a methodological advance that Schopenhauer never made.

## The Problem of Suffering

Schopenhauer's pessimistic verdict — in a world of endless striving, suffering is the norm and life cannot be justified — is the philosophical problem the *Genealogy* addresses. As Leiter emphasizes (2014: 44-46), the "revaluation of values" is Nietzsche's attempt to respond to Schopenhauer's challenge: if life involves senseless suffering, how can it be justified?

**In the Second Essay:** The essay's arc — from the animal pain of mnemotechnics (§3) through the cruelty of punishment (§5-6) to the self-torture of bad conscience (§16-18) to the theological maximization of suffering (§19-22) — can be read as a genealogy of Schopenhauer's problem. Suffering is not a timeless metaphysical fact but a historically produced and constantly reinterpreted phenomenon.

§23's Greek gods absorb guilt precisely to prevent suffering from becoming *meaningful* in Schopenhauer's sense — suffering as evidence of cosmic guilt. The Greeks manage suffering through foolishness, not sin. The Christian solution — suffering as deserved punishment — is the ascetic ideal's answer to Schopenhauer's challenge, and Nietzsche considers it the worst possible answer because it makes suffering *worse* by adding guilt.

## Compassion (Mitleid)

Schopenhauer's ethics center on *Mitleid* (pity/compassion) as the only genuinely moral motive — because in compassion we transcend the illusion of individuality and recognize the unity of the will. Nietzsche announces in the *Genealogy*'s Preface that he must "confront my great teacher Schopenhauer" on "the value of the 'unegoistic,' the instincts of compassion [*Mitleid*], self-denial, self-sacrifice" (GM Pref:5).

**In the Second Essay:** The attack on compassion is mostly implicit, but it underlies the essay's revaluation of cruelty. Schopenhauer treats cruelty as the opposite of moral value; Nietzsche insists cruelty is constitutive of human development (§6-7). The "good conscience of cruelty" (§7) — the pre-modern capacity to enjoy suffering without guilt — is precisely what Schopenhauer's ethics would condemn. See [[cruelty-and-festival]].

## Spinoza and the *Morsus Conscientiae* (§15)

§15's invocation of Spinoza's *morsus conscientiae* has a Schopenhauerian dimension. Schopenhauer engaged extensively with Spinoza, and both shared a deterministic framework. Nietzsche uses Spinoza to show that even the "sting of conscience" is not moral guilt but regret at miscalculation — "something has unexpectedly gone wrong," not "I ought not to have done that." This Spinozist point reinforces the Schopenhauerian denial of moral responsibility: if actions follow necessarily from character and circumstance, then the appropriate response to failure is not guilt but recalibration.
