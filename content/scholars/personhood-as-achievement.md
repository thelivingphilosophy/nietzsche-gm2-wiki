# Personhood as Achievement

Gemes's most radical and distinctive claim (2006: 327-336): for Nietzsche, being a *person* is not a universal endowment but a **rare achievement**. Most humans are not genuine persons — they are "merely passive conduits for various disparate forces already existing and operating around them" (2006: 332). Genuine personhood requires imposing a unified, hierarchical order on one's drives, so that there is a real "I" that acts rather than a jumble of impulses that happen to inhabit the same body.

## The Argument

### Most Humans as "Jumbles of Drives"

Nietzsche's drive psychology (developed across BGE, GS, D, and the *Genealogy*) holds that the self is not a simple unity but a "social structure of drives and affects" (BGE 12). Most people inherit contradictory drives from multiple cultural and biological sources:

> "In the present age human beings have in their bodies the heritage of multiple origins, that is opposite and not merely opposite drives and value standards that fight each other and rarely permit each other any rest." (BGE 200)

These "hybrid" moderns are internally fragmented: "what becomes sickest and degenerates most in such hybrids is the *will*: they no longer know independence of decisions and the intrepid sense of pleasure in willing" (BGE 208). Without a dominant organizing drive, there is no stable self — just a shifting assemblage that is pushed around by whatever impulse is strongest at the moment.

### The Sovereign Individual as Genuine Person

The [[sovereign-individual]] of §2 is, on Gemes's reading, one of the few who has achieved genuine personhood. His "own independent, protracted will" indicates a stable hierarchy of drives, unified under a dominant organizing principle. He can promise because his will *persists* across time — not because an external system enforces his commitments, but because there is a genuine "I" that remains the same from the moment of promising to the moment of fulfillment.

This is why the sovereign individual promises "rarely, slowly" — he knows the weight of a promise, because for him it is a commitment of a *real self*, not the cheap word of a jumble of drives that may feel differently tomorrow.

### Nietzsche's Nachlass Supports This

From a notebook entry of the *Genealogy* period: "One should not at all assume that many humans are 'people' [*Personen*]. ... the 'person' is a relatively isolated fact" (KSA 12:491, quoted in Gemes 2006: 328). Personhood is a "relatively isolated fact" — rare, exceptional, not the default condition.

## Personhood and Bad Conscience

Gemes's reading connects directly to the Second Essay's central mechanism:

**Bad conscience creates interiority** (§16) — the "interior world" that was previously "as thin as if it were stretched between two membranes" gains "depth, breadth, and height." But interiority alone is not personhood. You can have a deep interior world that is still a chaos of competing drives. Bad conscience is a *necessary condition* for personhood (you need interiority to have a self at all) but not a *sufficient* one (you also need hierarchical organization of that interiority).

The process: (1) the morality of mores breeds memory and calculability (§1-3); (2) social confinement creates interiority through bad conscience (§16-18); (3) the rare individual *organizes* that interiority into a unified self (§2). Steps 1 and 2 happen to most humans. Step 3 happens to almost nobody.

This reframes the relationship between the sovereign individual and bad conscience: bad conscience is not the sovereign's *opposite* but his *raw material*. The sovereign individual is what you get when the creative illness of bad conscience is *mastered* rather than succumbing to religious moralization.

## The Normative Dimension

If most humans are not genuine persons, disturbing implications follow:

- **Moral responsibility.** If agency requires genuine selfhood, and most people lack it, then most people are not fully responsible for their actions — not because "free will doesn't exist" (the metaphysical argument) but because most people don't have a genuine self from which to act (the psychological argument). This is more radical than standard determinism.

- **The status of the herd.** The herd — those who are "merely passive conduits" — are not contemptible but simply *incomplete*. They are human organisms that haven't achieved personhood. This is Nietzsche's elitism in its sharpest form, but Gemes frames it as descriptive rather than normative: he's not saying the herd *shouldn't* exist, but that their mode of existence is fundamentally different from the sovereign individual's.

- **Promising.** The [[memory-and-promising|morality of mores]] makes *everyone* capable of keeping promises through external enforcement. But only the genuine person can make a *real* promise — one backed by a stable self rather than by fear of punishment. This maps onto the three kinds of promise in the [[sovereign-individual]] page.

## Tensions

- **Is this testable?** How do we know who is a "genuine person" and who is a "jumble of drives"? The criterion seems to be internal coherence and unified will, but these are hard to assess from the outside.
- **Does this collapse into Leiter's fatalism?** If whether you achieve personhood depends on your type-facts, then personhood is not really an "achievement" — it's a lucky natural endowment. Gemes resists this: he argues that while type-facts are necessary, the *organization* of drives into a hierarchy involves a kind of self-creation that goes beyond passive expression of natural constitution.
- **What about partial personhood?** Gemes presents a binary (person vs. jumble), but most people seem to have *some* degree of unified selfhood. Is there a spectrum? Nietzsche's language sometimes suggests one — "strong and weak wills" (BGE 21) implies degrees rather than a binary.
